Chapter 8: Exergy

This chapter builds significantly on the energy and entropy concepts from previous chapters, introducing a powerful way to assess the quality of energy and the true potential for work.

8.1 EXERGY, REVERSIBLE WORK, AND IRREVERSIBILITY

Motivation: We've previously looked at efficiencies (like turbine or compressor efficiency) which compare an actual device to an idealized reversible version operating between specific pressures. However, we want a more general concept to evaluate the maximum possible work we can get from any system or process, considering its interaction with the surrounding environment. This leads us to Exergy.

What is Exergy?

  • Verbally: Exergy is the maximum theoretical useful work (shaft work or electrical work) obtainable from a system as it interacts with the environment and comes into complete equilibrium with it.
  • The Environment: The environment is typically considered a large body at a constant pressure P₀ and temperature T₀ (often called the "dead state"). It acts as an infinite source/sink for heat and can absorb any volume changes without changing its own state.
  • Symbol: Exergy is often denoted by Φ (for total exergy) or φ (for specific exergy).
  • Initial Definition: Φ = Wout given an ambient P₀, T₀.
  • Relationship to Reversible Work: Exergy is very closely related to the reversible work (Wrev) we discussed earlier. Reversible work is the maximum work obtainable during a process between specific start and end states, possibly interacting with heat reservoirs. Exergy is the specific case where the final state is the dead state (P₀, T₀).

Maximum Work from a Heat Source Q

  1. Constant Temperature Reservoir at T (Figure 8.1):
    • How can we get work from a heat source Q coming from a reservoir at temperature T? We use a heat engine.
    • To get the maximum work, the heat engine must be reversible.
    • Since the engine must interact with the heat source T and also the ambient environment at T₀ (our reference), the ideal engine operates between these two temperatures.
    • A reversible engine operating between two constant temperatures is a Carnot heat engine.
    • Energy Balance for Carnot HE: Wrev HE = QH - QL = Q - Q₀ (using QH = Q).
    • Entropy Balance for Reversible Cycle: ∮ δQ/T = 0, so QH/TH - QL/TL = 0. In our case, Q/T - Q₀/T₀ = 0.
    • Solving for Q₀: Q₀ = Q * (T₀ / T).
    • Substituting Q₀ into the energy balance gives the maximum reversible work, which is the exergy of the heat Q:
    ΦQT = Wrev HE = Q - Q * (T₀ / T)
    ΦQT = Q * (1 - T₀ / T)    (Eq 8.1)
    • Interpretation (Figure 8.2): On a T-S diagram, the heat Q transferred at constant T is represented by the area of a rectangle with height T and width ΔS = Q/T. The exergy ΦQT is the portion of this area *above* the ambient temperature T₀. The area below T₀ (which is T₀ * ΔS = T₀ * (Q/T) = Q₀) represents the heat that *must* be rejected to the environment even by the best possible engine and is therefore unavailable energy. Exergy represents the available energy portion of the heat transfer Q. Only the fraction (1 - T₀/T) (which is the Carnot efficiency) of the heat Q can be converted to work.
  2. Changing Temperature Source (Figure 8.3):
    • What if the heat Q comes from a source whose temperature changes (like hot gas cooling down in a heat exchanger)?
    • We can imagine using a series of infinitesimally small Carnot engines operating between the changing source temperature T and the ambient T₀.
    • The total heat rejected to the ambient is now an integral: Q₀ = ∫ δQ₀ = ∫ T₀ (δQ / T) (from δQ/T - δQ₀/T₀ = 0 for each tiny engine).
    • If the process transferring the heat δQ is reversible, δQ/T = dS, where dS is the entropy change *of the source*.
    • So, Q₀ = ∫ T₀ dS = T₀ ∫ dS = T₀ ΔS (where ΔS is the total entropy change *of the source* providing the heat Q). (Equation 8.2, note ΔS = ∫ δQrev / T).
    Q₀ = T₀ ΔS    (Eq 8.2)
    • The total work is again Wrev HE = Q - Q₀. Therefore, the exergy of the heat Q from a changing-temperature source is:
    ΦQT = Wrev HE = Q - T₀ ΔS    (Eq 8.3)
    • Interpretation (Figure 8.3b): On a T-S diagram for the source fluid, Q is the total area under the process curve. T₀ ΔS is the area of the rectangle below T₀. ΦQT (the available energy) is the area under the curve *above* T₀.

Comparing Actual vs. Reversible Processes (General Control Volume - Figure 8.4)

  • Actual Process: Governed by standard conservation laws (Fig 8.4).
    dmcvdt = ∑ mi - ∑ me    (Eq 8.4)
    dEcvdt = ∑ Qj + ∑ mi htot,i - ∑ me htot,e - Wcv,ac    (Eq 8.5)
    dScvdt = ∑ QjTj + ∑ mi si - ∑ me se + Sgen,ac    (Eq 8.6)
    Note Ẇcv,ac is the actual work rate, Ṡgen,ac is the actual entropy generation rate (which is ≥ 0).
  • Ideal Reversible Process: We imagine an ideal counterpart that has the same control volume, same mass flows (ṁᵢ, ṁₑ at same states), same heat transfers (Q̇ⱼ at same Tⱼ), and same storage effects (dEcv/dt, dScv/dt). The key differences are:
    1. Entropy Generation: Ṡgen,rev = 0 (by definition of reversible).
    2. Work Term: It produces the reversible work rate Ẇrev.
    3. Extra Heat Transfer: To make the entropy balance work with Ṡgen=0, we must allow for a possible reversible heat transfer Q̇₀rev with the ambient environment at T₀. This term replaces the T₀Ṡgen,ac "lost work" potential.

Deriving the Reversible Work Rate (Ẇrev)

  1. Look at the entropy equation (8.6) for the actual process. Solve for Ṡgen,ac:
    Sgen,ac = dScvdt - ∑ QjTj - ∑ mi si + ∑ me se
  2. In the *ideal* reversible process, instead of generating Ṡgen,ac, this same amount of entropy must be transferred *out* reversibly to the environment at T₀. So, the necessary heat transfer *from* the environment (at T₀) *to* the ideal CV is:
    Q₀rev = T₀ Sgen,ac    (from Eq 8.7/8.9)
    Important Note: If the actual process is adiabatic (Q̇ⱼ=0) but irreversible (Ṡgen,ac>0), the corresponding *reversible* process needs heat transfer *from* the environment (Q̇₀rev > 0) to maintain the same state changes and flows reversibly. If the actual process is reversible (Ṡgen,ac=0), then Q̇₀rev = 0.
  3. The *total* work rate term in the ideal reversible process energy equation must replace the actual work -Ẇcv,ac. Comparing terms from the actual and ideal last parts (Eq 8.8): -Ẇcv,ac is replaced by (Q̇₀rev - Ẇrev).
  4. Solving for Ẇrev:
    Wrev = Wcv,ac + Q₀rev    (Eq 8.10)
  5. Now substitute Q̇₀rev = T₀ Ṡgen,ac: Ẇrev = Ẇcv,ac + T₀ Ṡgen,ac.
  6. Next, substitute the expression for Ṡgen,ac (from step 1) into this equation:
    Wrev = Wcv,ac + T₀ [ dScvdt - ∑QjTj - ∑mi si + ∑me se ]
  7. Now, replace Ẇcv,ac using the energy equation (8.5) solved for Ẇcv,ac:
    Wcv,ac = ∑Qj + ∑mi htot,i - ∑me htot,e - dEcvdt
  8. Plug this into the equation from step 6 (results in a long expression).
  9. Finally, group terms by heat transfer, mass flows, and storage:
    10. Wrev = ∑ Qj(1 - T₀/Tj) + ∑ mi(htot,i - T₀si) - ∑ me(htot,e - T₀se) - ( dEcvdt - T₀dScvdt )    (Eq 8.11)
    This equation gives the maximum theoretical (reversible) work rate obtainable from the given control volume process, considering its interactions with heat sources, mass flows, and internal storage changes, all relative to the ambient temperature T₀.

Irreversibility (İ) or Lost Work:

  • The difference between the best possible work (Ẇrev) and the actual work produced (Ẇcv,ac) is called the irreversibility rate (İ).
    I = Wrev - Wcv,ac    (Eq 8.12)
  • From Eq 8.10, we also know Ẇrev - Ẇcv,ac = Q̇₀rev.
  • From Eq 8.9 (related), we also know Q̇₀rev = T₀ Ṡgen,ac.
  • Therefore:
    I = Q₀rev = T₀ Sgen,ac    (Eq 8.13)
  • Conclusion: Irreversibility (lost work potential) is directly proportional to the rate of entropy generation, scaled by the ambient temperature T₀. Minimizing entropy generation maximizes useful work output (or minimizes required work input). Energy is conserved, but the potential to do work (exergy) is destroyed by irreversibility.
  • Figure 8.5: Shows this graphically. If actual work Ẇac,1 is positive (e.g., turbine), the reversible work Ẇrev,1 is higher by the amount İ₁. If actual work Ẇac,2 is negative (work input, e.g., compressor), the reversible work Ẇrev,2 is less negative (closer to zero) by the amount İ₂. Ẇrev is always algebraically greater than Ẇac.

Application to Steady-State Process (SISO):

  • In steady state, dEcv/dt = 0 and dScv/dt = 0. The last term in Eq 8.11 drops out.
  • For single inlet/outlet, Σṁᵢ(...) becomes ṁ(htotᵢ - T₀sᵢ) and Σṁₑ(...) becomes ṁ(htotₑ - T₀sₑ).
  • The reversible work rate is Ẇrev = ṁ [ Σqⱼ(1-T₀/Tⱼ) + (htotᵢ - T₀sᵢ) - (htotₑ - T₀sₑ) ].
  • Specific reversible work wrev = Ẇrev / ṁ:
    wrev = ∑qj(1 - T₀/Tj) + (htot,i - T₀si) - (htot,e - T₀se)    (Eq 8.14)
  • Specific irreversibility i = İ / ṁ:
    i = wrev - wac
    i = T₀ sgen,ac (where sgen,ac = Ṡgen,ac / ṁ)
    From entropy balance sₑ = sᵢ + Σqⱼ/Tⱼ + sgen, so sgen = sₑ - sᵢ - Σqⱼ/Tⱼ.
    i = T₀ [ se - si - ∑(qjTj) ]    (Eq 8.15)

Application to Control Mass Process:

  • No mass flows (ṁᵢ=ṁₑ=0). Eq 8.11 simplifies to:
    Wrev = ∑ Qj(1 - T₀/Tj) - ( dEcvdt - T₀dScvdt )    (Eq 8.16 rate form)
  • Integrate over time for a process from state 1 to 2:
    ₁W₂rev = Σ ∫Q̇ⱼ(1 - T₀/Tⱼ)dt - [(E₂ - E₁) - T₀(S₂ - S₁)].
    If heat ₁Q₂ⱼ comes from a reservoir at Tⱼ, ∫ Q̇ⱼ dt = ₁Q₂ⱼ:
    ₁W₂rev = ∑ ₁Q₂ⱼ(1 - T₀/Tj) - [ (E₂ - E₁) - T₀(S₂ - S₁) ]    (Eq 8.17)
  • Irreversibility for the process ₁I₂:
    ₁I₂ = ₁W₂rev - ₁W₂ac
    ₁I₂ = T₀ · ₁S₂,gen,ac    (Eq 8.18, first part)
    Substitute entropy generation from integrated entropy balance S₂ - S₁ = Σ ∫(Q̇ⱼ/Tⱼ) dt + ₁S₂,gen. Assume ₁Q₂ⱼ comes from Tⱼ.
    S₂ - S₁ = Σ (₁Q₂ⱼ / Tⱼ) + ₁S₂,gen => ₁S₂,gen = (S₂ - S₁) - Σ (₁Q₂ⱼ / Tⱼ).
    ₁I₂ = T₀ [ (S₂ - S₁) - ∑ (₁Q₂ⱼTj) ]    (Eq 8.18, second part)
  • If KE/PE negligible, E=U.

8.2 EXERGY AND ITS BALANCE EQUATION

This section formally defines Exergy based on the maximum work potential relative to the environment (dead state) and develops a balance equation for exergy flow and destruction.

  • Maximum Reversible Work & Exergy: The maximum possible work from a system in a given state occurs when it undergoes a reversible process to reach the dead state (equilibrium with the environment at P₀, T₀, zero velocity, reference elevation Z₀). This maximum work potential is the Exergy (Φ) of the initial state.
  • Exergy Components: We re-examine the terms in the reversible work equation (Eq 8.11) and identify them as different forms of exergy transfer or change, all measured relative to the dead state P₀, T₀.
    1. Exergy Transfer with Heat (Φ̇q):
      Φq = ∑ Qj (1 - T₀/Tj)    (Eq 8.21)
      This is the work potential of heat Q̇ⱼ coming from temperature Tⱼ. It's the portion of heat that *could* be converted to work by a Carnot engine operating between Tⱼ and T₀. Higher Tⱼ means higher quality (higher exergy content) heat. Heat at T₀ has zero exergy.
    2. Flow Exergy (ψ): The maximum work associated with a unit mass of fluid flowing at a certain state, as it reversibly reaches the dead state. From Eq 8.14 applied between the state and the dead state 0:
      wrev = (htot - T₀s) - (htot₀ - T₀s₀). This reversible work *is* the flow exergy ψ.
      ψ = (h - T₀s + V²/2 + gZ) - (h₀ - T₀s₀ + V₀²/2 + gZ₀)
      Since V₀=0, Z₀=0 (by convention):
      ψ = (h - h₀) - T₀(s - s₀) + V²/2 + gZ    (Eq 8.22 generalized form)
      Difference in flow exergy between inlet i and exit e:
      ψᵢ - ψₑ = [(htotᵢ - T₀sᵢ) - (htot₀ - T₀s₀)] - [(htotₑ - T₀sₑ) - (htot₀ - T₀s₀)]
      ψi - ψe = (htot,i - T₀si) - (htot,e - T₀se)    (Eq 8.23)
      The constant dead state terms cancel. This matches the flow terms in the specific reversible work equation (8.14).
    3. Stored Exergy (Φ or φ): The maximum work potential stored within the control volume due to its state relative to the environment.
      • From Eq 8.11, the storage term contribution to *rate* of reversible work is -(dEcv/dt - T₀ dScv/dt).
      • However, we must account for work done *on the surroundings* at P₀ if the CV volume changes (V). Ẇsurr = P₀ (dVcv/dt). This isn't useful work.
      • The maximum *available* work rate from storage changes is:
        Wavailmax = - ( dEcvdt - T₀dScvdt ) - P₀dVcvdt    (Eq 8.25 modified)
      • Define the rate of change of stored exergy Φ̇cv such that Ẇavailmax = -Φ̇cv.
        Φcv = dEcvdt - T₀ dScvdt + P₀ dVcvdt    (Eq 8.26)
      • Integrating from the dead state (E₀, S₀, V₀) to the actual state (E, S, V) gives the total stored exergy Φ:
        Φ = (E - E₀) - T₀(S - S₀) + P₀(V - V₀)
      • Specific stored exergy (φ for control mass): Divide by mass m, let E = U + KE + PE. Assume KE=PE=0 initially and at dead state. e = u.
        φ = (u - u₀) - T₀(s - s₀) + P₀(v - v₀)    (Eq 8.27 generalized)
      • Difference between two states:
        φ₂ - φ₁ = (u₂ - u₁) - T₀(s₂ - s₁) + P₀(v₂ - v₁)    (Eq 8.28)
        The dead state terms cancel.
  • Rewriting Reversible Work Rate using Exergy: Substitute the definitions Φ̇q, ψᵢ, ψₑ, and -Φ̇cv + P₀V̇ into Eq 8.11:
    Wrev = Φq + ∑miψi - ∑meψe - Φcv + P₀V    (Eq 8.29)
    (Here P₀V̇ = P₀ dV/dt)
  • The Exergy Balance Equation:
    1. Start with the link between actual and reversible work: Ẇac = Ẇrev - İ = Ẇrev - T₀Ṡgen.
    2. Substitute the expression for Ẇrev from Eq 8.29 into this.
    3. Rearrange to solve for the rate of change of stored exergy Φ̇cv:
      Φ̇cv = Φ̇q + Σṁᵢψᵢ - Σṁₑψₑ - (Ẇac - P₀V̇) - T₀Ṡgen
      (Recognize Ẇuseful = Ẇac - P₀V̇ as shaft/boundary work excluding work against atmosphere).
    4. Rewriting the Φ̇cv term (dΦcv/dt) and exergy flow with heat Φ̇q = ΣQ̇ⱼ(1-T₀/Tⱼ):
      dΦcvdt = ∑Qj(1-T₀/Tj) + ∑miψi - ∑meψe - (Wcv - P₀ dVdt) - T₀Sgen    (Eq 8.31)
  • Interpretation of Exergy Balance:
    (Rate of change of stored exergy) = (Rate of exergy transfer with heat) + (Rate of exergy transfer with flow IN) - (Rate of exergy transfer with flow OUT) - (Rate of useful work output) - (Rate of Exergy Destruction)
    • Exergy Destruction Rate = T₀ Ṡgen = İ. This term represents the rate at which the potential to do useful work is lost due to irreversibilities.
    • Unlike energy, exergy is *not* conserved; it is destroyed by entropy generation.

8.3 THE SECOND LAW EFFICIENCY

This section defines efficiency based on exergy ("Second Law Efficiency") rather than just energy ("First Law Efficiency").

  • Concept: Exergy represents the *true useful work potential* or the *quality* of energy. A Second Law efficiency compares the desired output (in exergy terms) to the required input (in exergy terms).
    η₂ = ηIId law = (Exergy Out / Exergy Used) = (Exergy Wanted / Exergy Supplied)
  • Device Efficiencies (Revisited):
    • Turbine:
      Wanted output: Actual work Ẇac. Exergy of work is work itself.
      Exergy used: The decrease in flow exergy from inlet to exit: ṁ(ψᵢ - ψₑ). This equals the reversible work Ẇrev *for the actual process end states*.
      η₂ = Wacm(ψi - ψe) = WacWrev    (Eq 8.32)
      Note: This uses the actual exit state e, unlike η₁ which used ideal state es. See Fig 8.12.
    • Compressor/Pump:
      Exergy wanted/gained: The increase in flow exergy ṁ(ψₑ - ψᵢ). This equals the magnitude of reversible work input |Ẇrev|.
      Exergy used: The actual work input |Ẇac|.
      η₂ = [ṁ(ψₑ - ψᵢ)] / |Ẇac| = |Ẇrev| / |Ẇac|
  • Heat Exchanger Efficiency (Fig 8.13):
    Exergy wanted: Increase in exergy of the cold stream ṁcold(ψ₂ - ψ₁).
    Exergy used: Decrease in exergy of the hot stream ṁhot(ψ₃ - ψ₄).
    η₂ = mcold(ψ₂ - ψ₁)mhot(ψ₃ - ψ₄)    (Eq 8.33)
  • General Formulation (using Exergy Destruction İ):
    Φsource = Total rate of exergy input (from flows, heat, work).
    Ẇac (or Φwanted) = Useful exergy output/effect.
    Wac = Φsource - Icv    (Eq 8.34)
    General Efficiency: η₂ = Φwanted / Φsource
    η₂ = Φsource - IcvΦsource = 1 - IcvΦsource    (Eq 8.35)
    Efficiency is reduced from 100% directly by the ratio of exergy destroyed to exergy supplied.
  • Cycle Efficiencies (Second Law Perspective):
    • Heat Engine:
      Work output WHE is the desired exergy output.
      Exergy input is from the heat source QH at TH: ΦH = QH (1 - T₀/TH).
      η2,HE = WHEΦH = WHEQH (1 - T₀/TH)    (Eq 8.36)
      Relationship to η₁ = WHE / QH: η₂ = η₁ / (1 - T₀/TH) = η₁ / ηCarnot(TH,T₀). η₂ is always ≥ η₁. It measures how well the engine performs compared to the best *possible* engine operating with that heat source relative to ambient.
    • Heat Pump:
      Desired exergy output (heating): The exergy value of the heat delivered QH at house temperature TH. ΦH = QH (1 - T₀/TH).
      Exergy input: Work input WHP (which is pure exergy).
      η2,HP = ΦHWHP = QH (1 - T₀/TH)WHP    (Eq 8.37)
      Relates to COP = QH / WHP: η₂ = COP * (1 - T₀/TH).
  • Exergy Destruction as Performance Measure: Analyzing where İ = T₀Ṡgen is largest shows where the biggest losses in potential occur. E.g., large ΔT between heat source and working fluid causes large İ. Condensers reject lots of energy but often at low T close to T₀, so the *exergy* loss is small.

8.4 ENGINEERING APPLICATIONS

  • Exergy analysis provides a deeper understanding than just energy analysis by accounting for the *quality* of energy and the *irreversibilities* that degrade performance.
  • Second Law efficiencies (η₂) compare actual performance to the *theoretically best possible* given the constraints and the environment (T₀), using the actual end states. First Law device efficiencies (η₁) compare actual to an idealized (usually isentropic) process ending at a different state than the actual one. η₂ is often higher than η₁.
  • The usefulness depends on experience – is 85% η₂ good? Depends on the device type and operating range.
  • Exergy analysis helps pinpoint *where* the largest losses (exergy destruction) occur in complex systems (like power plants), guiding efforts for improvement (e.g., minimizing ΔT in heat transfer).

In-Text Concept Questions Explained:

  • **(a-e from Chap 7):** Covered previously.
  • f. Energy can be stored as U, PE, KE. Are those forms all 100% available?

    No. Only forms of energy that can be *fully* converted to work in a reversible process are 100% available (i.e., are pure exergy). Work (shaft, electrical) is 100% exergy. KE and PE are also forms of organized energy and can ideally be fully converted to work, so they are 100% exergy relative to a zero KE/PE reference. Internal energy (U) is associated with temperature and microscopic disorder. Its potential to do work depends on its temperature relative to T₀ and its entropy. Only a *part* of U is available as exergy. Heat transfer is also not 100% available, as seen by the (1 - T₀/T) factor.

  • g. We cannot create or destroy energy. Can we create or destroy exergy?

    We cannot create exergy (that would violate the 2nd law, requiring Sgen < 0). We *can* (and always do in real processes) **destroy** exergy. Exergy destruction is İ = T₀ Sgen. Since Sgen ≥ 0, exergy is always decreasing or conserved (in the limit of reversible processes) for an isolated system.

  • h. In a turbine, what is the source of exergy?

    The source is the flow exergy (ψᵢ) of the high-pressure, high-temperature fluid entering the turbine. The turbine converts some of this flow exergy into shaft work exergy (Ẇac). Some is lost as exergy destruction (İ), and the remaining flow exergy (ψₑ) leaves with the exiting fluid.

  • i. In a pump, what is the source of exergy and what gains exergy?

    The source is the shaft work input (|Ẇac|), which is pure exergy. The fluid gains exergy (ṁ(ψₑ - ψᵢ)) as its pressure (and thus enthalpy and work potential) is increased. Some input exergy is also destroyed (İ).

  • j. In a heat engine, what is the source of exergy?

    The source is the exergy associated with the heat transfer from the high-temperature source (ΦH = QH(1 - T₀/TH)).

  • k. In a heat pump, what is the source of exergy?

    The source is the work input (WHP). (Although it also utilizes low-exergy heat QL from the cold reservoir).

  • l. In Eq 8.36, ΦH was written for heat transfer. What if source is hot gas being cooled?

    Eq 8.36: WHE = η₂ * ΦH = η₂ * [QH (1 - T₀/TH)].

    If the source is a hot gas stream (ṁgas) cooling from state 3 to state 4 to provide the energy Q̇H = ṁgas(h₃ - h₄) to the heat engine cycle, the exergy *input* is the decrease in the *flow exergy* of the gas:

    Φsource = mgas(ψ₃ - ψ₄) = mgas[(h₃ - h₄) - T₀(s₃ - s₄)]

    The second law efficiency would then be:

    η₂ = WHEmgas(ψ₃ - ψ₄)
