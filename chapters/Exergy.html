Chapter 8: Exergy

This chapter builds significantly on the energy and entropy concepts from previous chapters, introducing a powerful way to assess the quality of energy and the true potential for work.

8.1 EXERGY, REVERSIBLE WORK, AND IRREVERSIBILITY

Motivation: We've previously looked at efficiencies (like turbine or compressor efficiency) which compare an actual device to an idealized reversible version operating between specific pressures. However, we want a more general concept to evaluate the maximum possible work we can get from any system or process, considering its interaction with the surrounding environment. This leads us to Exergy.

What is Exergy?

  • Verbally: Exergy is the maximum theoretical useful work (shaft work or electrical work) obtainable from a system as it interacts with the environment and comes into complete equilibrium with it.
  • The Environment: The environment is typically considered a large body at a constant pressure $P_0$ and temperature $T_0$ (often called the "dead state"). It acts as an infinite source/sink for heat and can absorb any volume changes without changing its own state.
  • Symbol: Exergy is often denoted by $\Phi$ (for total exergy) or $\phi$ (for specific exergy).
  • Initial Definition: $\Phi = W_{out}$ given an ambient $P_0$, $T_0$.
  • Relationship to Reversible Work: Exergy is very closely related to the reversible work ($W_{rev}$) we discussed earlier. Reversible work is the maximum work obtainable during a process between specific start and end states, possibly interacting with heat reservoirs. Exergy is the specific case where the final state is the dead state ($P_0$, $T_0$).

Maximum Work from a Heat Source $Q$

  1. Constant Temperature Reservoir at $T$ (Figure 8.1):
    • How can we get work from a heat source $Q$ coming from a reservoir at temperature $T$? We use a heat engine.
    • To get the maximum work, the heat engine must be reversible.
    • Since the engine must interact with the heat source $T$ and also the ambient environment at $T_0$ (our reference), the ideal engine operates between these two temperatures.
    • A reversible engine operating between two constant temperatures is a Carnot heat engine.
    • Energy Balance for Carnot HE: $W_{rev HE} = Q_H - Q_L = Q - Q_0$ (using $Q_H = Q$).
    • Entropy Balance for Reversible Cycle: $\oint \delta Q/T = 0$, so $Q_H/T_H - Q_L/T_L = 0$. In our case, $Q/T - Q_0/T_0 = 0$.
    • Solving for $Q_0$: $Q_0 = Q \frac{T_0}{T}$.
    • Substituting $Q_0$ into the energy balance gives the maximum reversible work, which is the exergy of the heat $Q$:
    $$ \Phi_{QT} = W_{rev HE} = Q - Q \frac{T_0}{T} $$
    $$ \Phi_{QT} = Q (1 - \frac{T_0}{T}) \quad (Eq 8.1) $$
    • Interpretation (Figure 8.2): On a T-S diagram, the heat $Q$ transferred at constant T is represented by the area of a rectangle with height $T$ and width $\Delta S = Q/T$. The exergy $\Phi_{QT}$ is the portion of this area above the ambient temperature $T_0$. The area below $T_0$ (which is $T_0 \Delta S = T_0 (Q/T) = Q_0$) represents the heat that must be rejected to the environment even by the best possible engine and is therefore unavailable energy. Exergy represents the available energy portion of the heat transfer $Q$. Only the fraction $(1 - T_0/T)$ (which is the Carnot efficiency) of the heat $Q$ can be converted to work.
  2. Changing Temperature Source (Figure 8.3):
    • What if the heat $Q$ comes from a source whose temperature changes (like hot gas cooling down in a heat exchanger)?
    • We can imagine using a series of infinitesimally small Carnot engines operating between the changing source temperature $T$ and the ambient $T_0$.
    • The total heat rejected to the ambient is now an integral: $Q_0 = \int \delta Q_0 = \int T_0 (\delta Q / T)$ (from $\delta Q/T - \delta Q_0/T_0 = 0$ for each tiny engine).
    • If the process transferring the heat $\delta Q$ is reversible, $\delta Q/T = dS$, where $dS$ is the entropy change of the source.
    • So, $Q_0 = \int T_0 dS = T_0 \int dS = T_0 \Delta S$ (where $\Delta S$ is the total entropy change of the source providing the heat $Q$). (Equation 8.2, note $\Delta S = \int \delta Q_{rev} / T$).
    $$ Q_0 = T_0 \Delta S \quad (Eq 8.2) $$
    • The total work is again $W_{rev HE} = Q - Q_0$. Therefore, the exergy of the heat $Q$ from a changing-temperature source is:
    $$ \Phi_{QT} = W_{rev HE} = Q - T_0 \Delta S \quad (Eq 8.3) $$
    • Interpretation (Figure 8.3b): On a T-S diagram for the source fluid, $Q$ is the total area under the process curve. $T_0 \Delta S$ is the area of the rectangle below $T_0$. $\Phi_{QT}$ (the available energy) is the area under the curve above $T_0$.

Comparing Actual vs. Reversible Processes (General Control Volume - Figure 8.4)

  • Actual Process: Governed by standard conservation laws (Fig 8.4).
    $$ \frac{dm_{cv}}{dt} = \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i - \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e \quad (Eq 8.4) $$
    $$ \frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} = \sum_{j} \dot{Q}_j + \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i h_{tot,i} - \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e h_{tot,e} - \dot{W}_{cv,ac} \quad (Eq 8.5) $$
    $$ \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} = \sum_{j} \frac{\dot{Q}_j}{T_j} + \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i s_i - \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e s_e + \dot{S}_{gen,ac} \quad (Eq 8.6) $$
    Note $\dot{W}_{cv,ac}$ is the actual work rate, $\dot{S}_{gen,ac}$ is the actual entropy generation rate (which is $\ge 0$).
  • Ideal Reversible Process: We imagine an ideal counterpart that has the same control volume, same mass flows ($\dot{m}_i$, $\dot{m}_e$ at same states), same heat transfers ($\dot{Q}_j$ at same $T_j$), and same storage effects ($dE_{cv}/dt$, $dS_{cv}/dt$). The key differences are:
    1. Entropy Generation: $\dot{S}_{gen,rev} = 0$ (by definition of reversible).
    2. Work Term: It produces the reversible work rate $\dot{W}_{rev}$.
    3. Extra Heat Transfer: To make the entropy balance work with $\dot{S}_{gen}=0$, we must allow for a possible reversible heat transfer $\dot{Q}_0^{rev}$ with the ambient environment at $T_0$. This term replaces the $T_0 \dot{S}_{gen,ac}$ "lost work" potential.

Deriving the Reversible Work Rate ($\dot{W}_{rev}$)

  1. Look at the entropy equation (8.6) for the actual process. Solve for $\dot{S}_{gen,ac}$:
    $$ \dot{S}_{gen,ac} = \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} - \sum_{j} \frac{\dot{Q}_j}{T_j} - \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i s_i + \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e s_e $$
  2. In the ideal reversible process, instead of generating $\dot{S}_{gen,ac}$, this same amount of entropy must be transferred out reversibly to the environment at $T_0$. So, the necessary heat transfer from the environment (at $T_0$) to the ideal CV is:
    $$ \dot{Q}_0^{rev} = T_0 \dot{S}_{gen,ac} \quad (from Eq 8.7/8.9) $$
    Important Note: If the actual process is adiabatic ($\dot{Q}_j=0$) but irreversible ($\dot{S}_{gen,ac}>0$), the corresponding *reversible* process needs heat transfer from the environment ($\dot{Q}_0^{rev} > 0$) to maintain the same state changes and flows reversibly. If the actual process is reversible ($\dot{S}_{gen,ac}=0$), then $\dot{Q}_0^{rev} = 0$.
  3. The total work rate term in the ideal reversible process energy equation must replace the actual work $-\dot{W}_{cv,ac}$. Comparing terms from the actual and ideal last parts (Eq 8.8): $-\dot{W}_{cv,ac}$ is replaced by $(\dot{Q}_0^{rev} - \dot{W}_{rev})$.
  4. Solving for $\dot{W}_{rev}$:
    $$ \dot{W}_{rev} = \dot{W}_{cv,ac} + \dot{Q}_0^{rev} \quad (Eq 8.10) $$
  5. Now substitute $\dot{Q}_0^{rev} = T_0 \dot{S}_{gen,ac}$: $\dot{W}_{rev} = \dot{W}_{cv,ac} + T_0 \dot{S}_{gen,ac}$.
  6. Next, substitute the expression for $\dot{S}_{gen,ac}$ (from step 1) into this equation:
    $$ \dot{W}_{rev} = \dot{W}_{cv,ac} + T_0 \left[ \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} - \sum_{j}\frac{\dot{Q}_j}{T_j} - \sum_{i}\dot{m}_i s_i + \sum_{e}\dot{m}_e s_e \right] $$
  7. Now, replace $\dot{W}_{cv,ac}$ using the energy equation (8.5) solved for $\dot{W}_{cv,ac}$:
    $$ \dot{W}_{cv,ac} = \sum_{j}\dot{Q}_j + \sum_{i}\dot{m}_i h_{tot,i} - \sum_{e}\dot{m}_e h_{tot,e} - \frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} $$
  8. Plug this into the equation from step 6 (results in a long expression).
  9. Finally, group terms by heat transfer, mass flows, and storage:
    10. $$ \dot{W}_{rev} = \sum_{j} \dot{Q}_j(1 - \frac{T_0}{T_j}) + \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i(h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i) - \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e(h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e) - \left( \frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} - T_0 \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} \right) \quad (Eq 8.11) $$
    This equation gives the maximum theoretical (reversible) work rate obtainable from the given control volume process, considering its interactions with heat sources, mass flows, and internal storage changes, all relative to the ambient temperature $T_0$.

Irreversibility ($\dot{I}$) or Lost Work:

  • The difference between the best possible work ($\dot{W}_{rev}$) and the actual work produced ($\dot{W}_{cv,ac}$) is called the irreversibility rate ($\dot{I}$).
    $$ \dot{I} = \dot{W}_{rev} - \dot{W}_{cv,ac} \quad (Eq 8.12) $$
  • From Eq 8.10, we also know $\dot{W}_{rev} - \dot{W}_{cv,ac} = \dot{Q}_0^{rev}$.
  • From Eq 8.9 (related), we also know $\dot{Q}_0^{rev} = T_0 \dot{S}_{gen,ac}$.
  • Therefore:
    $$ \dot{I} = \dot{Q}_0^{rev} = T_0 \dot{S}_{gen,ac} \quad (Eq 8.13) $$
  • Conclusion: Irreversibility (lost work potential) is directly proportional to the rate of entropy generation, scaled by the ambient temperature $T_0$. Minimizing entropy generation maximizes useful work output (or minimizes required work input). Energy is conserved, but the potential to do work (exergy) is destroyed by irreversibility.
  • Figure 8.5: Shows this graphically. If actual work $\dot{W}_{ac,1}$ is positive (e.g., turbine), the reversible work $\dot{W}_{rev,1}$ is higher by the amount $\dot{I}_1$. If actual work $\dot{W}_{ac,2}$ is negative (work input, e.g., compressor), the reversible work $\dot{W}_{rev,2}$ is less negative (closer to zero) by the amount $\dot{I}_2$. $\dot{W}_{rev}$ is always algebraically greater than $\dot{W}_{ac}$.

Application to Steady-State Process (SISO):

  • In steady state, $dE_{cv}/dt = 0$ and $dS_{cv}/dt = 0$. The last term in Eq 8.11 drops out.
  • For single inlet/outlet, $\sum \dot{m}_i(...) $ becomes $\dot{m}(h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i)$ and $\sum \dot{m}_e(...) $ becomes $\dot{m}(h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e)$.
  • The reversible work rate is $\dot{W}_{rev} = \dot{m} [ \sum_j q_j(1-\frac{T_0}{T_j}) + (h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i) - (h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e) ]$.
  • Specific reversible work $w_{rev} = \dot{W}_{rev} / \dot{m}$:
    $$ w_{rev} = \sum_{j} q_j(1 - \frac{T_0}{T_j}) + (h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i) - (h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e) \quad (Eq 8.14) $$
  • Specific irreversibility $i = \dot{I} / \dot{m}$:
    $i = w_{rev} - w_{ac}$
    $i = T_0 s_{gen,ac}$ (where $s_{gen,ac} = \dot{S}_{gen,ac} / \dot{m}$)
    From entropy balance $s_e = s_i + \sum_j q_j/T_j + s_{gen}$, so $s_{gen} = s_e - s_i - \sum_j q_j/T_j$.
    $$ i = T_0 \left[ s_e - s_i - \sum_{j} \frac{q_j}{T_j} \right] \quad (Eq 8.15) $$

Application to Control Mass Process:

  • No mass flows ($\dot{m}_i=\dot{m}_e=0$). Eq 8.11 simplifies to:
    $$ \dot{W}_{rev} = \sum_{j} \dot{Q}_j(1 - \frac{T_0}{T_j}) - \left( \frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} - T_0 \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} \right) \quad (Eq 8.16 \text{ rate form}) $$
  • Integrate over time for a process from state 1 to 2:
    ${_1W_2^{rev}} = \sum_j \int \dot{Q}_j(1 - T_0/T_j)dt - [(E_2 - E_1) - T_0(S_2 - S_1)]$.
    If heat ${_1Q_{2j}}$ comes from a reservoir at $T_j$, $\int \dot{Q}_j dt = {_1Q_{2j}}$:
    $$ {_1W_2^{rev}} = \sum_{j} {_1Q_{2j}}(1 - \frac{T_0}{T_j}) - \left[ (E_2 - E_1) - T_0(S_2 - S_1) \right] \quad (Eq 8.17) $$
  • Irreversibility for the process ${_1I_2}$:
    ${_1I_2} = {_1W_2^{rev}} - {_1W_2^{ac}}$
    $$ {_1I_2} = T_0 \cdot {_1S_{2,gen,ac}} \quad (Eq 8.18, \text{ first part}) $$
    Substitute entropy generation from integrated entropy balance $S_2 - S_1 = \sum_j \int(\dot{Q}_j/T_j) dt + {_1S_{2,gen}}$. Assume ${_1Q_{2j}}$ comes from $T_j$.
    $S_2 - S_1 = \sum_j ({_1Q_{2j}} / T_j) + {_1S_{2,gen}}$ => ${_1S_{2,gen}} = (S_2 - S_1) - \sum_j ({_1Q_{2j}} / T_j)$.
    $$ {_1I_2} = T_0 \left[ (S_2 - S_1) - \sum_{j} \frac{{_1Q_{2j}}}{T_j} \right] \quad (Eq 8.18, \text{ second part}) $$
  • If KE/PE negligible, $E=U$.

Application to Transient Process:

  • Integrate Eq 8.11 over time $t$:
    $$ {_1W_2^{rev}} = \sum_{j} \int \dot{Q}_j(1-\frac{T_0}{T_j})dt + \sum_{i} \int \dot{m}_i(h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i)dt - \sum_{e} \int \dot{m}_e(h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e)dt - [(E_2-E_1) - T_0(S_2-S_1)]_{cv} \quad (Eq 8.19 \text{ generalized}) $$
  • If heat ${_1Q_{2j}}$ comes from reservoir $T_j$, inlet flows $m_i$ total come from state $i$, exit flows $m_e$ total go to state $e$:
    ${_1W_2^{rev}} = \sum_j {_1Q_{2j}}(1-\frac{T_0}{T_j}) + \sum_i m_i(h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i) - \sum_e m_e(h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e) - [(E_2-E_1) - T_0(S_2-S_1)]_{cv}$
  • Irreversibility ${_1I_2}$:
    $$ {_1I_2} = {_1W_2^{rev}} - {_1W_2^{ac}} = T_0 \cdot {_1S_{2,gen,ac}} \quad (Eq 8.20) $$

8.2 EXERGY AND ITS BALANCE EQUATION

This section formally defines Exergy based on the maximum work potential relative to the environment (dead state) and develops a balance equation for exergy flow and destruction.

  • Maximum Reversible Work & Exergy: The maximum possible work from a system in a given state occurs when it undergoes a reversible process to reach the dead state (equilibrium with the environment at $P_0$, $T_0$, zero velocity, reference elevation $Z_0$). This maximum work potential is the Exergy ($\Phi$) of the initial state.
  • Exergy Components: We re-examine the terms in the reversible work equation (Eq 8.11) and identify them as different forms of exergy transfer or change, all measured relative to the dead state $P_0, T_0$.
    1. Exergy Transfer with Heat ($\dot{\Phi}_q$):
      $$ \dot{\Phi}_q = \sum_{j} \dot{Q}_j (1 - \frac{T_0}{T_j}) \quad (Eq 8.21) $$
      This is the work potential of heat $\dot{Q}_j$ coming from temperature $T_j$. It's the portion of heat that *could* be converted to work by a Carnot engine operating between $T_j$ and $T_0$. Higher $T_j$ means higher quality (higher exergy content) heat. Heat at $T_0$ has zero exergy.
    2. Flow Exergy ($\psi$): The maximum work associated with a unit mass of fluid flowing at a certain state, as it reversibly reaches the dead state. From Eq 8.14 applied between the state and the dead state 0:
      $w_{rev} = (h_{tot} - T_0 s) - (h_{tot,0} - T_0 s_0)$. This reversible work *is* the flow exergy $\psi$.
      $\psi = (h - T_0 s + \frac{V^2}{2} + gZ) - (h_0 - T_0 s_0 + \frac{V_0^2}{2} + gZ_0)$
      Since $V_0=0$, $Z_0=0$ (by convention):
      $$ \psi = (h - h_0) - T_0(s - s_0) + \frac{V^2}{2} + gZ \quad (Eq 8.22 \text{ generalized form}) $$
      Difference in flow exergy between inlet $i$ and exit $e$:
      $\psi_i - \psi_e = [(h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i) - (h_{tot,0} - T_0 s_0)] - [(h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e) - (h_{tot,0} - T_0 s_0)]$
      $$ \psi_i - \psi_e = (h_{tot,i} - T_0 s_i) - (h_{tot,e} - T_0 s_e) \quad (Eq 8.23) $$
      The constant dead state terms cancel. This matches the flow terms in the specific reversible work equation (8.14).
    3. Stored Exergy ($\Phi$ or $\phi$): The maximum work potential stored within the control volume due to its state relative to the environment.
      • From Eq 8.11, the storage term contribution to *rate* of reversible work is $-(\frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} - T_0 \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt})$.
      • However, we must account for work done *on the surroundings* at $P_0$ if the CV volume changes ($V$). $\dot{W}_{surr} = P_0 \frac{dV_{cv}}{dt}$. This isn't useful work.
      • The maximum available work rate from storage changes is:
        $$ \dot{W}_{avail}^{max} = - \left( \frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} - T_0 \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} \right) - P_0 \frac{dV_{cv}}{dt} \quad (Eq 8.25 \text{ modified}) $$
      • Define the rate of change of stored exergy $\dot{\Phi}_{cv}$ such that $\dot{W}_{avail}^{max} = -\dot{\Phi}_{cv}$.
        $$ \dot{\Phi}_{cv} = \frac{dE_{cv}}{dt} - T_0 \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} + P_0 \frac{dV_{cv}}{dt} \quad (Eq 8.26) $$
      • Integrating from the dead state ($E_0, S_0, V_0$) to the actual state ($E, S, V$) gives the total stored exergy $\Phi$:
        $\Phi = (E - E_0) - T_0(S - S_0) + P_0(V - V_0)$
      • Specific stored exergy ($\phi$ for control mass): Divide by mass $m$, let $E = U + KE + PE$. Assume KE=PE=0 initially and at dead state. $e = u$.
        $$ \phi = (u - u_0) - T_0(s - s_0) + P_0(v - v_0) \quad (Eq 8.27 \text{ generalized}) $$
      • Difference between two states:
        $$ \phi_2 - \phi_1 = (u_2 - u_1) - T_0(s_2 - s_1) + P_0(v_2 - v_1) \quad (Eq 8.28) $$
        The dead state terms cancel.
  • Rewriting Reversible Work Rate using Exergy: Substitute the definitions $\dot{\Phi}_q$, $\psi_i$, $\psi_e$, and $-\dot{\Phi}_{cv} + P_0 \dot{V}$ into Eq 8.11:
    $$ \dot{W}_{rev} = \dot{\Phi}_q + \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i \psi_i - \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e \psi_e - \dot{\Phi}_{cv} + P_0 \dot{V} \quad (Eq 8.29) $$
    (Here $P_0 \dot{V} = P_0 dV/dt$)
  • The Exergy Balance Equation:
    1. Start with the link between actual and reversible work: $\dot{W}_{ac} = \dot{W}_{rev} - \dot{I} = \dot{W}_{rev} - T_0 \dot{S}_{gen}$.
    2. Substitute the expression for $\dot{W}_{rev}$ from Eq 8.29 into this.
    3. Rearrange to solve for the rate of change of stored exergy $\dot{\Phi}_{cv}$:
      $\dot{\Phi}_{cv} = \dot{\Phi}_q + \sum_i \dot{m}_i \psi_i - \sum_e \dot{m}_e \psi_e - (\dot{W}_{ac} - P_0 \dot{V}) - T_0 \dot{S}_{gen}$
      (Recognize $\dot{W}_{useful} = \dot{W}_{ac} - P_0 \dot{V}$ as shaft/boundary work excluding work against atmosphere).
    4. Rewriting the $\dot{\Phi}_{cv}$ term ($d\Phi_{cv}/dt$) and exergy flow with heat $\dot{\Phi}_q = \sum_j \dot{Q}_j(1-T_0/T_j)$:
      $$ \frac{d\Phi_{cv}}{dt} = \sum_{j} \dot{Q}_j(1-\frac{T_0}{T_j}) + \sum_{i} \dot{m}_i \psi_i - \sum_{e} \dot{m}_e \psi_e - (\dot{W}_{cv} - P_0 \frac{dV}{dt}) - T_0 \dot{S}_{gen} \quad (Eq 8.31) $$
  • Interpretation of Exergy Balance:
    (Rate of change of stored exergy) = (Rate of exergy transfer with heat) + (Rate of exergy transfer with flow IN) - (Rate of exergy transfer with flow OUT) - (Rate of useful work output) - (Rate of Exergy Destruction)
    • Exergy Destruction Rate $= T_0 \dot{S}_{gen} = \dot{I}$. This term represents the rate at which the potential to do useful work is lost due to irreversibilities.
    • Unlike energy, exergy is not conserved; it is destroyed by entropy generation.

8.3 THE SECOND LAW EFFICIENCY

This section defines efficiency based on exergy ("Second Law Efficiency") rather than just energy ("First Law Efficiency").

  • Concept: Exergy represents the true useful work potential or the quality of energy. A Second Law efficiency compares the desired output (in exergy terms) to the required input (in exergy terms).
    $\eta_2 = \eta_{IId law} = \frac{\text{Exergy Out}}{\text{Exergy Used}} = \frac{\text{Exergy Wanted}}{\text{Exergy Supplied}}$
  • Device Efficiencies (Revisited):
    • Turbine:
      Wanted output: Actual work $\dot{W}_{ac}$. Exergy of work is work itself.
      Exergy used: The decrease in flow exergy from inlet to exit: $\dot{m}(\psi_i - \psi_e)$. This equals the reversible work $\dot{W}_{rev}$ for the actual process end states.
      $$ \eta_2 = \frac{\dot{W}_{ac}}{\dot{m}(\psi_i - \psi_e)} = \frac{\dot{W}_{ac}}{\dot{W}_{rev}} \quad (Eq 8.32) $$
      Note: This uses the actual exit state $e$, unlike $\eta_1$ which used ideal state $es$. See Fig 8.12.
    • Compressor/Pump:
      Exergy wanted/gained: The increase in flow exergy $\dot{m}(\psi_e - \psi_i)$. This equals the magnitude of reversible work input $|\dot{W}_{rev}|$.
      Exergy used: The actual work input $|\dot{W}_{ac}|$.
      $\eta_2 = \frac{\dot{m}(\psi_e - \psi_i)}{|\dot{W}_{ac}|} = \frac{|\dot{W}_{rev}|}{|\dot{W}_{ac}|}$
  • Heat Exchanger Efficiency (Fig 8.13):
    Exergy wanted: Increase in exergy of the cold stream $\dot{m}_{cold}(\psi_2 - \psi_1)$.
    Exergy used: Decrease in exergy of the hot stream $\dot{m}_{hot}(\psi_3 - \psi_4)$.
    $$ \eta_2 = \frac{\dot{m}_{cold}(\psi_2 - \psi_1)}{\dot{m}_{hot}(\psi_3 - \psi_4)} \quad (Eq 8.33) $$
  • General Formulation (using Exergy Destruction $\dot{I}$):
    $\Phi_{source}$ = Total rate of exergy input (from flows, heat, work).
    $\dot{W}_{ac}$ (or $\Phi_{wanted}$) = Useful exergy output/effect.
    $$ \dot{W}_{ac} = \Phi_{source} - \dot{I}_{cv} \quad (Eq 8.34) $$
    General Efficiency: $\eta_2 = \Phi_{wanted} / \Phi_{source}$
    $$ \eta_2 = \frac{\Phi_{source} - \dot{I}_{cv}}{\Phi_{source}} = 1 - \frac{\dot{I}_{cv}}{\Phi_{source}} \quad (Eq 8.35) $$
    Efficiency is reduced from 100% directly by the ratio of exergy destroyed to exergy supplied.
  • Cycle Efficiencies (Second Law Perspective):
    • Heat Engine:
      Work output $W_{HE}$ is the desired exergy output.
      Exergy input is from the heat source $Q_H$ at $T_H$: $\Phi_H = Q_H (1 - T_0/T_H)$.
      $$ \eta_{2,HE} = \frac{W_{HE}}{\Phi_H} = \frac{W_{HE}}{Q_H (1 - \frac{T_0}{T_H})} \quad (Eq 8.36) $$
      Relationship to $\eta_1 = W_{HE} / Q_H$: $\eta_2 = \eta_1 / (1 - T_0/T_H) = \eta_1 / \eta_{Carnot}(T_H,T_0)$. $\eta_2$ is always $\ge \eta_1$. It measures how well the engine performs compared to the best *possible* engine operating with that heat source relative to ambient.
    • Heat Pump:
      Desired exergy output (heating): The exergy value of the heat delivered $Q_H$ at house temperature $T_H$. $\Phi_H = Q_H (1 - T_0/T_H)$.
      Exergy input: Work input $W_{HP}$ (which is pure exergy).
      $$ \eta_{2,HP} = \frac{\Phi_H}{W_{HP}} = \frac{Q_H (1 - \frac{T_0}{T_H})}{W_{HP}} \quad (Eq 8.37) $$
      Relates to $COP = Q_H / W_{HP}$: $\eta_2 = COP \cdot (1 - T_0/T_H)$.
  • Exergy Destruction as Performance Measure: Analyzing where $\dot{I} = T_0 \dot{S}_{gen}$ is largest shows where the biggest losses in potential occur. E.g., large $\Delta T$ between heat source and working fluid causes large $\dot{I}$. Condensers reject lots of energy but often at low $T$ close to $T_0$, so the exergy loss is small.

8.4 ENGINEERING APPLICATIONS

  • Exergy analysis provides a deeper understanding than just energy analysis by accounting for the quality of energy and the irreversibilities that degrade performance.
  • Second Law efficiencies ($\eta_2$) compare actual performance to the theoretically best possible given the constraints and the environment ($T_0$), using the actual end states. First Law device efficiencies ($\eta_1$) compare actual to an idealized (usually isentropic) process ending at a different state than the actual one. $\eta_2$ is often higher than $\eta_1$.
  • The usefulness depends on experience – is 85% $\eta_2$ good? Depends on the device type and operating range.
  • Exergy analysis helps pinpoint where the largest losses (exergy destruction) occur in complex systems (like power plants), guiding efforts for improvement (e.g., minimizing $\Delta T$ in heat transfer).

In-Text Concept Questions Explained:

  • **(a-e from Chap 7):** Covered previously.
  • f. Energy can be stored as U, PE, KE. Are those forms all 100% available?

    No. Only forms of energy that can be fully converted to work in a reversible process are 100% available (i.e., are pure exergy). Work (shaft, electrical) is 100% exergy. KE and PE are also forms of organized energy and can ideally be fully converted to work, so they are 100% exergy relative to a zero KE/PE reference. Internal energy (U) is associated with temperature and microscopic disorder. Its potential to do work depends on its temperature relative to $T_0$ and its entropy. Only a part of $U$ is available as exergy. Heat transfer is also not 100% available, as seen by the $(1 - T_0/T)$ factor.

  • g. We cannot create or destroy energy. Can we create or destroy exergy?

    We cannot create exergy (that would violate the 2nd law, requiring $S_{gen} < 0$). We can (and always do in real processes) destroy exergy. Exergy destruction is $\dot{I} = T_0 S_{gen}$. Since $S_{gen} \ge 0$, exergy is always decreasing or conserved (in the limit of reversible processes) for an isolated system.

  • h. In a turbine, what is the source of exergy?

    The source is the flow exergy ($\psi_i$) of the high-pressure, high-temperature fluid entering the turbine. The turbine converts some of this flow exergy into shaft work exergy ($\dot{W}_{ac}$). Some is lost as exergy destruction ($\dot{I}$), and the remaining flow exergy ($\psi_e$) leaves with the exiting fluid.

  • i. In a pump, what is the source of exergy and what gains exergy?

    The source is the shaft work input ($|\dot{W}_{ac}|$), which is pure exergy. The fluid gains exergy ($\dot{m}(\psi_e - \psi_i)$) as its pressure (and thus enthalpy and work potential) is increased. Some input exergy is also destroyed ($\dot{I}$).

  • j. In a heat engine, what is the source of exergy?

    The source is the exergy associated with the heat transfer from the high-temperature source ($\Phi_H = Q_H(1 - T_0/T_H)$).

  • k. In a heat pump, what is the source of exergy?

    The source is the work input ($W_{HP}$). (Although it also utilizes low-exergy heat $Q_L$ from the cold reservoir).

  • l. In Eq 8.36, ΦH was written for heat transfer. What if source is hot gas being cooled?

    Eq 8.36: $W_{HE} = \eta_2 \Phi_H = \eta_2 [Q_H (1 - T_0/T_H)]$.

    If the source is a hot gas stream ($\dot{m}_{gas}$) cooling from state 3 to state 4 to provide the energy $\dot{Q}_H = \dot{m}_{gas}(h_3 - h_4)$ to the heat engine cycle, the exergy input is the decrease in the flow exergy of the gas:

    $$ \Phi_{source} = \dot{m}_{gas}(\psi_3 - \psi_4) = \dot{m}_{gas}[(h_3 - h_4) - T_0(s_3 - s_4)] $$

    The second law efficiency would then be:

    $$ \eta_2 = \frac{W_{HE}}{\dot{m}_{gas}(\psi_3 - \psi_4)} $$

This concludes the detailed walkthrough of Chapter 8. Exergy gives us the tools to analyze the quality of energy, the true potential for work, and where this potential is lost in real processes.
