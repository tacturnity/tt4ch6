Question 1: Air Conditioner Performance

Question:

A window air conditioner, as shown in the figure, rejects $6.2$ kW of heat ($\dot{Q}_H$) to the ambient surroundings while consuming $1.8$ kW of electrical power ($\dot{W}_{in}$). Determine the rate of cooling provided to the cool space ($\dot{Q}_L$) and the Coefficient of Performance (COP) of the air conditioner.

Final Answer:
  • Rate of cooling ($\dot{Q}_L$) = $4.4$ kW
  • Coefficient of Performance (COP) = $2.44$
Full Solution:

1. Energy Balance:

An air conditioner (refrigerator) is a cyclic device. Applying the first law (energy balance) for a cycle operating in steady state: Energy In = Energy Out.

  • Energy inputs: $\dot{W}_{in}$ and $\dot{Q}_L$.
  • Energy output: $\dot{Q}_H$.
$$ \dot{W}_{in} + \dot{Q}_L = \dot{Q}_H $$

2. Calculate Rate of Cooling ($\dot{Q}_L$):

  • Given: $\dot{W}_{in} = 1.8$ kW, $\dot{Q}_H = 6.2$ kW
$$ \dot{Q}_L = \dot{Q}_H - \dot{W}_{in} $$ $$ \dot{Q}_L = 6.2 \text{ kW} - 1.8 \text{ kW} = 4.4 \text{ kW} $$

3. Calculate Coefficient of Performance (COP):

COP for a refrigerator (air conditioner) is defined as desired output (cooling) divided by required input (work).

$$ COP_R = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{\dot{W}_{in}} $$ $$ COP_R = \frac{4.4 \text{ kW}}{1.8 \text{ kW}} = 2.444... \approx 2.44 $$

Question 2: Heat Pump Minimum COP

Question:

A heat pump driven by a $1.5$ kW motor is used to maintain a chicken hatchery at $35$°C. The hatchery loses heat to the colder ambient surroundings at a rate of $9$ kW. What is the minimum Coefficient of Performance (COP) that the heat pump must have to meet the heating requirement?

Final Answer:

The minimum acceptable COP is $6.0$.

Full Solution:

1. Identify Needs: The heat pump must supply heat ($\dot{Q}_H$) equal to the heat loss.

  • Required $\dot{Q}_H = 9$ kW

2. Identify Input: Assuming motor power is work input to the cycle:

  • $\dot{W}_{in} = 1.5$ kW

3. Calculate Minimum COP:

COP of a heat pump: $COP_{HP} = \dot{Q}_H / \dot{W}_{in}$. The minimum acceptable COP is the one required to achieve the necessary heating rate with the available work input.

$$ \text{Minimum Acceptable } COP_{HP} = \frac{\text{Required } \dot{Q}_H}{\text{Available } \dot{W}_{in}} $$ $$ \text{Minimum } COP_{HP} = \frac{9 \text{ kW}}{1.5 \text{ kW}} = 6.0 $$

Question 3: Diesel Engine Exhaust Flow

Question:

A large stationary diesel engine produces $15$ MW of power with a thermal efficiency of $40\%$. The exhaust gas, assumed to be air, exits the engine at $800$ K. The intake air is at $290$ K. Assuming the heat rejection occurs solely through the cooling of the exhaust gas down to the intake temperature, calculate the mass flow rate of the exhaust gas. Additionally, comment on the potential usability of the exhaust flow energy.

Final Answer:
  • Mass flow rate $\dot{m} \approx 42.3$ kg/s.
  • Yes, the exhaust flow energy can be used (high temperature).
Full Solution:

1. Engine Performance:

  • $\dot{W}_{out} = 15$ MW $= 15000$ kW
  • $\eta_{th} = 40\% = 0.40$

2. Calculate Heat Input ($\dot{Q}_H$):

$$ \eta_{th} = \frac{\dot{W}_{out}}{\dot{Q}_H} \implies \dot{Q}_H = \frac{\dot{W}_{out}}{\eta_{th}} $$ $$ \dot{Q}_H = \frac{15 \text{ MW}}{0.40} = 37.5 \text{ MW} = 37500 \text{ kW} $$

3. Calculate Heat Rejected ($\dot{Q}_L$):

$$ \dot{Q}_H = \dot{W}_{out} + \dot{Q}_L \implies \dot{Q}_L = \dot{Q}_H - \dot{W}_{out} $$ $$ \dot{Q}_L = 37.5 \text{ MW} - 15 \text{ MW} = 22.5 \text{ MW} = 22500 \text{ kW} $$

4. Relate Heat Rejection to Exhaust Cooling:

Assume $\dot{Q}_L$ is entirely due to exhaust cooling from $T_{exhaust}=800$ K to $T_{intake}=290$ K.

$$ \dot{Q}_L = \dot{m} (h_{exhaust} - h_{intake}) $$

5. Get Enthalpy Values (Table A.7.1, Air):

  • $h(800 \text{ K}) = 822.20$ kJ/kg
  • $h(290 \text{ K}) = 290.43$ kJ/kg

6. Calculate Mass Flow Rate ($\dot{m}$):

$$ \dot{m} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{h_{exhaust} - h_{intake}} $$ $$ \dot{m} = \frac{22500 \text{ kW}}{(822.20 - 290.43) \text{ kJ/kg}} = \frac{22500}{531.77} \frac{\text{kJ/s}}{\text{kJ/kg}} $$ $$ \dot{m} = 42.31 \text{ kg/s} \approx 42.3 \text{ kg/s} $$

7. Usability of Exhaust Energy:

Yes. The exhaust gas at $800$ K is significantly hotter than the ambient, containing substantial thermal energy (high enthalpy) that represents a potential for further work extraction (e.g., bottoming cycle) or for heating purposes.

Table Values Used Summary: A.7.1 (Air enthalpies).

Question 4: Heat Pump Minimum Power

Question:

A large heat pump is required to "upgrade" $5$ MW of heat available at $85$°C to be delivered as useful heating at $150$°C. What is the minimum theoretical power input required to drive this heat pump?

Final Answer:

The minimum power input is $0.907$ MW (or $907$ kW).

Full Solution:

1. Identify Process & Temperatures: Heat pump transferring heat $Q_L$ from $T_L$ to $T_H$.

  • Low-temperature source: $T_L = 85$°C $= 358.15$ K
  • High-temperature sink (delivery): $T_H = 150$°C $= 423.15$ K

2. Heat Flow: "Upgrade 5 MW of heat *at* 85°C" implies the heat *extracted* from the source is $5$ MW.

  • $\dot{Q}_L = 5$ MW

3. Minimum Work (Reversible Cycle): Corresponds to a Carnot heat pump operating between $T_L$ and $T_H$.

The Coefficient of Performance for the equivalent reversible *refrigerator* operating between these temperatures is:

$$ COP_{R,rev} = \frac{T_L}{T_H - T_L} = \frac{358.15 \text{ K}}{(423.15 - 358.15) \text{ K}} = \frac{358.15}{65.0} = 5.51 $$

4. Calculate Minimum Work Input ($\dot{W}_{in,min}$):

The definition of refrigerator COP relates the heat removed from the cold space ($\dot{Q}_L$) to the work input ($\dot{W}_{in}$). For the minimum work (reversible case):

$$ COP_{R,rev} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{\dot{W}_{in,min}} $$ $$ \dot{W}_{in,min} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{COP_{R,rev}} $$ $$ \dot{W}_{in,min} = \frac{5 \text{ MW}}{5.51} = 0.9074 \text{ MW} \approx 0.907 \text{ MW} $$

Question 5: Thermal Storage & Heat Engine

Question:

A thermal storage system uses a $2$ m³ bed of granite rock. The rock bed is heated to $400$ K using solar energy. A heat engine operates using the rock bed as the high-temperature source ($\dot{Q}_H$) and rejects heat ($\dot{Q}_L$) to the ambient environment at $290$ K. As the engine operates, the rock bed cools down, and the process stops when the rock bed reaches $290$ K. Determine:
a) The total energy (heat) the rock bed can supply to the heat engine.
b) The heat engine efficiency at the beginning of the process (when rock is $400$ K).
c) The heat engine efficiency at the end of the process (when rock is $290$ K).

Final Answer:

a) Total Energy supplied $Q_{H,total} \approx 538$ MJ.
b) Initial Efficiency $\eta_{initial} \approx 27.5\%$.
c) Final Efficiency $\eta_{final} = 0\%$.

Full Solution:

1. Rock Bed Properties:

  • Volume $V = 2$ m³
  • Density $\rho \approx 2750$ kg/m³ (Table A.3)
  • Specific Heat $C_p \approx 0.89$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.3)
  • Mass $m = \rho V = 2750 \times 2 = 5500$ kg

2. Temperatures:

  • Initial rock temp $T_{H,initial} = 400$ K
  • Final rock temp $T_{H,final} = 290$ K
  • Ambient (low temp sink) $T_L = 290$ K

3. Total Energy Supplied ($Q_{H,total}$):

Heat transferred *from* the rock bed as it cools.

$$ Q_{H,total} = m C_p (T_{H,initial} - T_{H,final}) $$ $$ Q_{H,total} = 5500 \text{ kg} \times 0.89 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times (400 - 290) \text{ K} $$ $$ Q_{H,total} = 4895 \times 110 = 538450 \text{ kJ} \approx 538 \text{ MJ} $$

4. Initial Heat Engine Efficiency ($\eta_{initial}$):

Maximum possible (Carnot) efficiency uses instantaneous source temperature $T_H$. At the beginning, $T_H = T_{H,initial} = 400$ K.

$$ \eta_{initial} = 1 - \frac{T_L}{T_{H,initial}} = 1 - \frac{290 \text{ K}}{400 \text{ K}} = 1 - 0.725 = 0.275 = 27.5\% $$

5. Final Heat Engine Efficiency ($\eta_{final}$):

At the end, $T_H = T_{H,final} = 290$ K.

$$ \eta_{final} = 1 - \frac{T_L}{T_{H,final}} = 1 - \frac{290 \text{ K}}{290 \text{ K}} = 1 - 1 = 0 = 0\% $$

Table Values Used Summary: A.3 (Granite density and Cp).

Question 6: R-134a Cooling and Freezer Work

Question:

A steel bottle with a volume $V = 0.1$ m³ contains R-134a initially at $T_1 = 20$°C and $P_1 = 200$ kPa. The bottle is placed inside a deep freezer where it cools down to $T_2 = -20$°C. The freezer operates within a room where the ambient temperature is $T_{amb} = 20$°C, and the freezer maintains an inside temperature of $T_{frez} = -20$°C. Find:
a) The amount of energy (heat $Q_L$) that must be removed from the R-134a.
b) The minimum amount of work input ($W_{min}$) required for the freezer to accomplish this cooling process.
State any assumptions clearly.

Final Answer:

a) Heat removed from R-134a, $Q_L \approx 62.3$ kJ.
b) Minimum work input to freezer, $W_{min} \approx 9.84$ kJ.

Full Solution:

Assumptions:

  • R-134a is the system undergoing a constant volume process.
  • Freezer operates as a cyclic device between $T_{frez}$ and $T_{amb}$.
  • Minimum work corresponds to a reversible (Carnot) refrigeration cycle.

1. Initial State (State 1):

  • $T_1 = 20$°C, $P_1 = 200$ kPa. State is superheated vapor (Table B.5.2).
  • $v_1 = 0.11436$ m³/kg, $u_1 = 395.27$ kJ/kg.

2. Calculate Mass (m):

$$ m = \frac{V}{v_1} = \frac{0.1 \text{ m³}}{0.11436 \text{ m³/kg}} = 0.8744 \text{ kg} $$

3. Final State (State 2):

  • $T_2 = -20$°C, $v_2 = v_1 = 0.11436$ m³/kg.
  • Check Table B.5.1 at -20°C: $v_f = 0.000738$, $v_g = 0.14649$. Since $v_f < v_2 < v_g$, state 2 is saturated mixture.

4. Calculate Quality ($x_2$) and Internal Energy ($u_2$):

  • $v_{fg} = v_g - v_f = 0.14649 - 0.000738 = 0.145752$ m³/kg
  • $u_{fg} = 192.85$ kJ/kg (from table)
$$ x_2 = \frac{v_2 - v_f}{v_{fg}} = \frac{0.11436 - 0.000738}{0.145752} = \frac{0.113622}{0.145752} = 0.77956 $$ $$ u_2 = u_f + x_2 u_{fg} = 173.65 + 0.77956 \times 192.85 = 173.65 + 150.39 = 324.04 \text{ kJ/kg} $$

5. Calculate Heat Removed from R-134a ($Q_L$):

First Law for R-134a (constant volume, W=0): $Q_{out} = U_1 - U_2 = m(u_1 - u_2)$.

$$ Q_L = Q_{out} = 0.8744 \text{ kg} \times (395.27 - 324.04) \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} $$ $$ Q_L = 0.8744 \times 71.23 = 62.27 \text{ kJ} \approx 62.3 \text{ kJ} $$

6. Calculate Minimum Freezer Work ($W_{min}$):

  • Freezer removes $Q_L = 62.27$ kJ.
  • $T_L = T_{frez} = -20$°C $= 253.15$ K
  • $T_H = T_{amb} = 20$°C $= 293.15$ K

Minimum work for a reversible refrigerator (Carnot):

$$ COP_{R,rev} = \frac{T_L}{T_H - T_L} = \frac{253.15}{293.15 - 253.15} = \frac{253.15}{40.0} = 6.32875 $$ $$ W_{min} = \frac{Q_L}{COP_{R,rev}} = \frac{62.27 \text{ kJ}}{6.32875} = 9.839 \text{ kJ} \approx 9.84 \text{ kJ} $$

Table Values Used Summary: B.5.1, B.5.2 (R-134a).

Question 7: Air Carnot Cycle Performance

Question:

An ideal gas Carnot cycle using air in a piston-cylinder has a high temperature of $T_H = 1200$ K and rejects heat at a low temperature of $T_L = 400$ K. During the isothermal heat addition process (1->2), the volume triples ($V_2 = 3V_1$). Find the specific heat added ($q_H$), the specific heat rejected ($q_L$), and the overall cycle efficiency ($\eta_{th}$).

Final Answer:
  • $\eta_{th} = 66.7\%$
  • $q_H \approx 378.4$ kJ/kg
  • $|q_L| \approx 126.1$ kJ/kg (magnitude of heat rejected)
Full Solution:

1. Cycle Efficiency:

Carnot efficiency depends only on absolute temperatures:

$$ \eta_{th} = 1 - \frac{T_L}{T_H} = 1 - \frac{400 \text{ K}}{1200 \text{ K}} = 1 - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.6667 = 66.7\% $$

2. Specific Heat Added ($q_H$):

Occurs during reversible isothermal expansion 1->2 at $T_H$. For ideal gas, $Q=W$ for isothermal process.

$$ q_H = w_{12} = \int_1^2 P dv = \int_1^2 \frac{RT_H}{v} dv = R T_H \ln(\frac{v_2}{v_1}) $$

Given $V_2 = 3V_1$, so $v_2 = 3v_1$. Use $R_{air} = 0.287$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5).

$$ q_H = 0.287 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times 1200 \text{ K} \times \ln(3) $$ $$ q_H = 344.4 \times 1.0986 = 378.38 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} \approx 378.4 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} $$

3. Specific Heat Rejected ($q_L$):

Occurs during reversible isothermal compression 3->4 at $T_L$. For ideal gas Carnot cycle, $v_2/v_1 = v_3/v_4$.

$$ |q_L| = w_{34} = R T_L \ln(\frac{v_3}{v_4}) = R T_L \ln(\frac{v_2}{v_1}) $$ $$ |q_L| = 0.287 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times 400 \text{ K} \times \ln(3) $$ $$ |q_L| = 114.8 \times 1.0986 = 126.12 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} \approx 126.1 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} $$

4. Check using Efficiency:

$$ \eta_{th} = \frac{W_{net}}{q_H} = \frac{q_H - |q_L|}{q_H} = \frac{378.38 - 126.12}{378.38} = \frac{252.26}{378.38} = 0.6666... $$

Matches efficiency calculated from temperatures.

Table Values Used Summary: A.5 (R for Air).

Question 8: Cycle Possibility Check

Question:

A cyclic machine receives $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW of heat from a reservoir at $T_H = 1000$ K and rejects $\dot{Q}_L = 125$ kJ? (*Assume Q_L=1.25kW based on W*) **Wait, the units are inconsistent. Let's assume Q_L = 1.25 kW** of heat to a reservoir at $T_L = 400$ K. The cycle produces $\dot{W}_{out} = 2$ kW? (*Assume W=2kW based on Q_H=6, Q_L=4?*) **Let's use the values given explicitly in part (a) from the source text: Q̇_H = 6 kW, Q̇_L = 4 kW, Ẇ = 2 kW.**
A cyclic machine receives $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW from a reservoir at $T_H = 1000$ K and rejects $\dot{Q}_L = 4$ kW to a reservoir at $T_L = 400$ K. The cycle produces $\dot{W}_{out} = 2$ kW of work output. Determine if this cycle is reversible, irreversible, or impossible. **(Original parts b, c, d omitted as only part a is relevant to the question text provided)**

Final Answer:

The cycle is **Irreversible** (but possible).

Full Solution:

1. Check First Law (Energy Conservation):

  • Given: $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 4$ kW (heat rejected), $\dot{W} = 2$ kW (output).

For a cycle: $\dot{Q}_H = \dot{W} + \dot{Q}_L$.

Check: $6 \text{ kW} = 2 \text{ kW} + 4 \text{ kW} \implies 6 \text{ kW} = 6 \text{ kW}$. **Satisfied.**

2. Check Second Law (Clausius Inequality):

For any cycle: $\oint (\frac{\delta Q}{T})_b \le 0$. For this cycle:

$$ \frac{\dot{Q}_H}{T_H} - \frac{|\dot{Q}_L|}{T_L} \le 0 $$
  • $T_H = 1000$ K
  • $T_L = 400$ K
$$ \frac{6 \text{ kW}}{1000 \text{ K}} - \frac{4 \text{ kW}}{400 \text{ K}} = 0.006 - 0.01 = -0.004 \frac{\text{kW}}{\text{K}} $$

Since $-0.004 < 0$, the Clausius Inequality is **satisfied**. The cycle is possible.

3. Determine Reversibility:

If the cycle were reversible, the Clausius sum would be exactly zero. Since it is less than zero ($-0.004$), the cycle is **Irreversible**.

(Alternatively, compare efficiency: $\eta = \dot{W} / \dot{Q}_H = 2/6 = 1/3 \approx 33.3\%$. $\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - T_L/T_H = 1 - 400/1000 = 0.6 = 60\%$. Since $\eta < \eta_{Carnot}$, the cycle is possible and irreversible).

Question 9: Engine Driven Air Conditioner

Question:

A car engine with $\eta_{eng} = 33\%$ drives an air conditioner (A/C) unit. On a day when the outside air (ambient) is at $35$°C, the A/C takes this air, cools it to $5$°C, and delivers it to a duct. The A/C unit requires $\dot{W}_{in} = 2$ kW of power input. The A/C's actual COP is half the COP of a corresponding Carnot refrigeration unit operating between the outside air temperature and the cooled air temperature. Find:
a) The extra rate of fuel energy ($\dot{Q}_{fuel,extra}$) consumed by the engine just to drive the A/C.
b) The actual COP of the A/C unit.
c) The mass flow rate ($\dot{m}_{air}$) of cold air the A/C unit provides.

Final Answer:

a) $\dot{Q}_{fuel,extra} \approx 6.06$ kW.
b) $COP_{actual} \approx 4.64$.
c) $\dot{m}_{air} \approx 0.308$ kg/s.

Full Solution:

1. Temperatures:

  • Outside air / High Temp for A/C: $T_H = 35$°C $= 308.15$ K
  • Cooled air / Low Temp for A/C: $T_L = 5$°C $= 278.15$ K

2. A/C Performance:

  • Work input: $\dot{W}_{in} = 2$ kW

Carnot COP (Refrigerator):

$$ COP_{R,Carnot} = \frac{T_L}{T_H - T_L} = \frac{278.15 \text{ K}}{(308.15 - 278.15) \text{ K}} = \frac{278.15}{30} = 9.2717 $$

Actual COP:

$$ COP_{actual} = 0.5 \times COP_{R,Carnot} = 0.5 \times 9.2717 = 4.63585 \approx 4.64 $$

3. Rate of Cooling ($\dot{Q}_L$):

$$ COP_{actual} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{\dot{W}_{in}} \implies \dot{Q}_L = COP_{actual} \times \dot{W}_{in} $$ $$ \dot{Q}_L = 4.63585 \times 2 \text{ kW} = 9.2717 \text{ kW} \approx 9.27 \text{ kW} $$

4. Extra Fuel Consumption:

$$ \eta_{eng} = \frac{\dot{W}_{in}}{\dot{Q}_{fuel,extra}} \implies \dot{Q}_{fuel,extra} = \frac{\dot{W}_{in}}{\eta_{eng}} $$ $$ \dot{Q}_{fuel,extra} = \frac{2 \text{ kW}}{0.33} = 6.0606 \text{ kW} \approx 6.06 \text{ kW} $$

5. Mass Flow Rate of Cooled Air:

The cooling load $\dot{Q}_L$ cools the air stream from $T_H$ to $T_L$. Use $C_p = 1.004$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5).

$$ \dot{Q}_L = \dot{m}_{air} C_p (T_H - T_L) \implies \dot{m}_{air} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{C_p (T_H - T_L)} $$ $$ \dot{m}_{air} = \frac{9.2717 \text{ kW}}{1.004 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times (35 - 5) \text{ K}} $$ $$ \dot{m}_{air} = \frac{9.2717}{1.004 \times 30} = \frac{9.2717}{30.12} = 0.3078 \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{s}} \approx 0.308 \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{s}} $$

Table Values Used Summary: A.5 (Cp for Air).

Question 11: A/C Maximum Ambient Temperature

Question:

An air conditioner maintains a house at $T_L = 20$°C using a maximum power input of $\dot{W}_{in,max} = 1.2$ kW. The house gains heat from the ambient surroundings at a rate of $\dot{Q}_{gain} = 0.6 \frac{\text{kW}}{\text{K}} \times (T_H - T_L)$, where $T_H$ is the outside ambient temperature. The actual COP of the air conditioner is $0.6$ times the theoretical maximum (Carnot) COP. Find the maximum outside temperature ($T_H$) for which the air conditioner can maintain the house at $20$°C.

Final Answer:

The maximum outside temperature is $T_H \approx 38.8$ °C ($311.9$ K).

Full Solution:

1. Cooling Requirement:**

To maintain $T_L = 20$°C $= 293.15$ K, A/C cooling rate $\dot{Q}_{L,cooling}$ must equal heat gain $\dot{Q}_{gain}$.

$$ \dot{Q}_{L,cooling} = \dot{Q}_{gain} = 0.6 \frac{\text{kW}}{\text{K}} \times (T_H - T_L) $$

2. Actual A/C Performance:**

$$ \dot{Q}_{L,cooling} = COP_{actual} \times \dot{W}_{in,max} $$

3. Relate Actual COP to Carnot COP:

$$ COP_{Carnot} = \frac{T_L}{T_H - T_L} $$ $$ COP_{actual} = 0.6 \times COP_{Carnot} = 0.6 \times \frac{T_L}{T_H - T_L} $$

4. Set up Equation:**

Set $\dot{Q}_{L,cooling}$ (from COP) equal to $\dot{Q}_{gain}$:

$$ \left[ 0.6 \times \frac{T_L}{T_H - T_L} \right] \times \dot{W}_{in,max} = 0.6 \frac{\text{kW}}{\text{K}} \times (T_H - T_L) $$

5. Solve for $T_H$:

Let $\Delta T = T_H - T_L$. Substitute known values ($T_L=293.15$ K, $\dot{W}_{in,max}=1.2$ kW).

$$ \left[ 0.6 \times \frac{293.15}{\Delta T} \right] \times 1.2 = 0.6 \times \Delta T $$

The $0.6$ factor cancels on both sides:

$$ \frac{293.15 \times 1.2}{\Delta T} = \Delta T $$ $$ (\Delta T)^2 = 293.15 \times 1.2 = 351.78 \text{ K}^2 $$ $$ \Delta T = \sqrt{351.78} = 18.756 \text{ K} $$ $$ T_H - T_L = 18.756 \text{ K} $$ $$ T_H = T_L + 18.756 \text{ K} = 293.15 \text{ K} + 18.756 \text{ K} = 311.906 \text{ K} $$ $$ T_H \approx 311.9 \text{ K} \approx 38.8 \text{ °C} $$

Table Values Used: None needed.

Question 12: Combined HE and Refrigerator

Question:

We want to provide refrigeration at $T_{cold} = -20$°C. We have a high-temperature reservoir at $T_{res} = 220$°C and the ambient temperature is $T_{amb} = 35$°C. A heat engine (HE) operates between $T_{res}$ and $T_{amb}$, and its work output is used to drive a refrigerator (Ref) operating between $T_{cold}$ and $T_{amb}$. Assuming all processes are reversible (Carnot cycles), determine the ratio of the heat transferred from the high-temperature reservoir ($\dot{Q}_{H1}$) to the heat transferred from the low-temperature refrigerated space ($\dot{Q}_L$).

Final Answer:

The ratio $\dot{Q}_{H1} / \dot{Q}_L$ is approximately $0.579$.

Full Solution:

1. Temperatures (Absolute):

  • $T_{res} = 220$°C $= 493.15$ K
  • $T_{amb} = 35$°C $= 308.15$ K
  • $T_{cold} = -20$°C $= 253.15$ K

2. Reversible Heat Engine (HE): Operates between $T_{res}$ and $T_{amb}$.

$$ \eta_{HE} = 1 - \frac{T_{amb}}{T_{res}} = 1 - \frac{308.15}{493.15} = 1 - 0.62488 = 0.37512 $$

Work output: $\dot{W} = \eta_{HE} \times \dot{Q}_{H1} = 0.37512 \times \dot{Q}_{H1}$

3. Reversible Refrigerator (Ref): Operates between $T_{cold}$ and $T_{amb}$.

$$ COP_R = \frac{T_{cold}}{T_{amb} - T_{cold}} = \frac{253.15}{308.15 - 253.15} = \frac{253.15}{55.0} = 4.6027 $$

Work input: $\dot{W} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{COP_R} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{4.6027}$

4. Equate Work: $\dot{W}_{HE} = \dot{W}_{Ref}$.

$$ 0.37512 \times \dot{Q}_{H1} = \frac{\dot{Q}_L}{4.6027} $$

5. Find Ratio $\dot{Q}_{H1} / \dot{Q}_L$:

$$ \frac{\dot{Q}_{H1}}{\dot{Q}_L} = \frac{1}{4.6027 \times 0.37512} = \frac{1}{1.72657} $$ $$ \frac{\dot{Q}_{H1}}{\dot{Q}_L} = 0.57919 \approx 0.579 $$

Question 13: Combined HE and HP using Waste Heat

Question:

A combined system consists of a heat engine driving a heat pump. Waste energy at $T_W = 50$°C is available. A portion of this waste energy ($\dot{Q}_{W1}$) acts as the heat source for the heat engine, which rejects heat at $T_L = 30$°C and produces work $\dot{W}$. The remaining waste energy ($\dot{Q}_{W2}$) serves as the heat source (low temperature) for the heat pump. The heat pump uses the work $\dot{W}$ produced by the engine and delivers heat $\dot{Q}_H$ at $T_H = 150$°C. The total waste energy available is $\dot{Q}_{W,total} = \dot{Q}_{W1} + \dot{Q}_{W2} = 5$ MW. Assuming both the heat engine and heat pump are reversible (Carnot), find the rate of energy delivered by the heat pump at the high temperature ($\dot{Q}_H$).

Final Answer:

The rate of energy delivered is $\dot{Q}_H \approx 1.09$ MW.

Full Solution:

1. Temperatures (Absolute):

  • Waste Source: $T_W = 50$°C $= 323.15$ K
  • HE Sink: $T_{L,HE} = 30$°C $= 303.15$ K
  • HP Sink (Delivery): $T_{H,HP} = 150$°C $= 423.15$ K
  • HP Source: $T_{source,HP} = T_W = 323.15$ K

2. System Configuration & Energy Flows:

  • $\dot{Q}_{W1} + \dot{Q}_{W2} = 5$ MW
  • Reversible HE: $\dot{W} = \dot{Q}_{W1} \eta_{HE} = \dot{Q}_{W1} (1 - T_{L,HE} / T_W)$
  • Reversible HP: $\dot{W} = \dot{Q}_H / COP_{HP} = \dot{Q}_H / [T_{H,HP} / (T_{H,HP} - T_{source,HP})]$

3. Calculate Efficiencies/COP:

  • $\eta_{HE} = 1 - 303.15 / 323.15 = 1 - 0.9381 = 0.06189$
  • $COP_{HP} = 423.15 / (423.15 - 323.15) = 423.15 / 100 = 4.2315$

4. Relate Heat Flows via Work:

$$ \dot{W} = 0.06189 \dot{Q}_{W1} $$ $$ \dot{W} = \frac{\dot{Q}_H}{4.2315} $$

Equating work: $0.06189 \dot{Q}_{W1} = \dot{Q}_H / 4.2315$

5. Relate Heat Flows via Energy Balance on HP:

$$ \dot{Q}_H = \dot{W} + \dot{Q}_{W2} $$

6. Solve the System of Equations:

Substitute $\dot{W}$ from HE into HP energy balance:

$$ \dot{Q}_H = (0.06189 \dot{Q}_{W1}) + \dot{Q}_{W2} $$

Use $\dot{Q}_{W2} = 5 - \dot{Q}_{W1}$:

$$ \dot{Q}_H = 0.06189 \dot{Q}_{W1} + (5 - \dot{Q}_{W1}) = 5 - 0.93811 \dot{Q}_{W1} $$

From work equality: $\dot{Q}_{W1} = \frac{\dot{Q}_H}{4.2315 \times 0.06189} = \frac{\dot{Q}_H}{0.26189}$

Substitute this $\dot{Q}_{W1}$ into the expression for $\dot{Q}_H$:

$$ \dot{Q}_H = 5 - 0.93811 \left( \frac{\dot{Q}_H}{0.26189} \right) $$ $$ \dot{Q}_H = 5 - 3.582 \dot{Q}_H $$ $$ \dot{Q}_H (1 + 3.582) = 5 $$ $$ 4.582 \dot{Q}_H = 5 $$ $$ \dot{Q}_H = \frac{5}{4.582} = 1.0912 \text{ MW} \approx 1.09 \text{ MW} $$

Question 14: Carnot Engine Optimal Temperature

Question:

A Carnot heat engine receives energy $\dot{Q}_H$ from a reservoir at temperature $T_{res}$ via a heat exchanger. The rate of heat transfer through the heat exchanger is proportional to the temperature difference: $\dot{Q}_H = K(T_{res} - T_H)$, where $T_H$ is the high temperature of the Carnot cycle and $K$ is a constant. The engine rejects heat $\dot{Q}_L$ at a fixed low temperature $T_L$. To maximize the work output $\dot{W}$ of the engine, show that the optimal high temperature $T_H$ for the cycle should be selected as $T_H = \sqrt{T_L T_{res}}$.

Final Answer:

The derivation showing $T_H = \sqrt{T_L T_{res}}$ for maximum work is presented in the solution.

Full Solution:

1. Work Output Expression:

Work output $\dot{W} = \eta_{Carnot} \times \dot{Q}_H$.

  • $\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_L}{T_H}$
  • $\dot{Q}_H = K (T_{res} - T_H)$
$$ \dot{W}(T_H) = \left(1 - \frac{T_L}{T_H}\right) K (T_{res} - T_H) $$

2. Expand Work Expression:

$$ \dot{W}(T_H) = K \left( T_{res} - T_H - \frac{T_L T_{res}}{T_H} + \frac{T_L T_H}{T_H} \right) $$ $$ \dot{W}(T_H) = K \left[ T_{res} - T_H - T_L T_{res} T_H^{-1} + T_L \right] $$

3. Maximize Work (Calculus):

Find the derivative of $\dot{W}$ with respect to $T_H$ and set it to zero. Treat $K, T_{res}, T_L$ as constants.

$$ \frac{d\dot{W}}{dT_H} = K \left[ 0 - 1 - T_L T_{res} (-1) T_H^{-2} + 0 \right] $$ $$ \frac{d\dot{W}}{dT_H} = K \left[ -1 + \frac{T_L T_{res}}{T_H^2} \right] $$

4. Set Derivative to Zero:

$$ K \left[ -1 + \frac{T_L T_{res}}{T_H^2} \right] = 0 $$

Since $K \neq 0$:

$$ -1 + \frac{T_L T_{res}}{T_H^2} = 0 $$ $$ 1 = \frac{T_L T_{res}}{T_H^2} $$ $$ T_H^2 = T_L T_{res} $$ $$ T_H = \sqrt{T_L T_{res}} \quad \text{(Q.E.D.)} $$

5. Conclusion: The work output is maximized when $T_H$ is the geometric mean of $T_L$ and $T_{res}$.

Question 15: Heat Engine Cycle Possibility

Question:

For each of the following cases describing a heat engine, determine if the First Law (Energy Conservation) is satisfied and if the Second Law is violated.
a) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 4$ kW, $\dot{W} = 2$ kW
b) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 0$ kW, $\dot{W} = 6$ kW
c) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = -2$ kW, $\dot{W} = -5$ kW
d) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 6$ kW, $\dot{W} = 0$ kW

Final Answer:

a) First Law: Satisfied. Second Law: Not violated (Possible Irreversible).
b) First Law: Satisfied. Second Law: Violated (unless $T_L = 0$ K).
c) First Law: Violated. Second Law: Cannot evaluate properly (Impossible by 1st Law).
d) First Law: Satisfied. Second Law: Not violated (Possible Irreversible).

Full Solution:

General Principles:

  • First Law: $\dot{Q}_H = \dot{W} + \dot{Q}_L$ (Assuming $\dot{Q}_L$ is magnitude rejected).
  • Second Law: Requires $\eta = \dot{W} / \dot{Q}_H \le \eta_{Carnot} < 1$ (for $T_L > 0$) and $\dot{Q}_L \ge 0$.

(a) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 4$ kW, $\dot{W} = 2$ kW

  • First Law: $6 = 2 + 4$? Yes. **Satisfied.**
  • Second Law: $\eta = 2/6 = 1/3$. Since $0 < \eta < 1$, this is possible if $T_L/T_H = 2/3$. $\dot{Q}_L > 0$. **Not violated.** (Possible Irreversible or Reversible)

(b) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 0$ kW, $\dot{W} = 6$ kW

  • First Law: $6 = 6 + 0$? Yes. **Satisfied.**
  • Second Law: $\eta = 6/6 = 1$. This violates the Kelvin-Planck statement (requires $\eta < 1$) unless the heat sink is at absolute zero ($T_L = 0$ K). **Violated (unless $T_L = 0$ K).**

(c) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = -2$ kW, $\dot{W} = -5$ kW

  • First Law: Does $6 = (-5) + (-2)$? No, $6 \neq -7$. **Violated.**
  • Second Law: Cannot evaluate meaningfully as energy is not conserved.

(d) $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW, $\dot{Q}_L = 6$ kW, $\dot{W} = 0$ kW

  • First Law: $6 = 0 + 6$? Yes. **Satisfied.**
  • Second Law: $\eta = 0/6 = 0$. This represents complete irreversibility (e.g., heat flowing directly from high to low T without work). It does not violate the principle that efficiency must be *less than or equal to* the Carnot efficiency (which is < 1). **Not violated.** (Possible Irreversible)

Question 16: Clausius Inequality Check

Question:

A heat engine receives $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW from a source at $T_H = 250$°C and rejects heat to a sink at $T_L = 30$°C. Examine the following three cases with respect to the inequality of Clausius:
(a) Work output $\dot{W} = 6$ kW
(b) Work output $\dot{W} = 0$ kW
(c) The engine operates as a Carnot cycle.

Final Answer:

(a) Ẇ=6kW: Violates Clausius Inequality (Impossible).
(b) Ẇ=0kW: Satisfies Clausius Inequality (Possible Irreversible).
(c) Carnot Cycle: Satisfies Clausius Inequality as equality (Reversible).

Full Solution:

1. Temperatures:

  • $T_H = 250$°C $= 523.15$ K
  • $T_L = 30$°C $= 303.15$ K

2. Inequality of Clausius: For a cycle operating between two reservoirs:

$$ \oint (\frac{\delta Q}{T})_b = \frac{\dot{Q}_H}{T_H} - \frac{|\dot{Q}_L|}{T_L} \le 0 $$

3. Heat Flows: $\dot{Q}_H = 6$ kW. First Law: $|\dot{Q}_L| = \dot{Q}_H - \dot{W}$.

(a) $\dot{W} = 6$ kW

  • $|\dot{Q}_L| = 6 - 6 = 0$ kW.
  • Clausius Check: $\frac{6}{523.15} - \frac{0}{303.15} = 0.01147 - 0 = +0.01147$ kW/K.
  • Since $> 0$, this **violates** the Clausius Inequality (Impossible).

(b) $\dot{W} = 0$ kW

  • $|\dot{Q}_L| = 6 - 0 = 6$ kW.
  • Clausius Check: $\frac{6}{523.15} - \frac{6}{303.15} = 0.01147 - 0.01979 = -0.00832$ kW/K.
  • Since $< 0$, this **satisfies** the Clausius Inequality (Possible Irreversible).

(c) Carnot Cycle

  • For a reversible Carnot cycle, the Clausius sum is exactly zero. $\oint (\delta Q/T)_b = 0$.
  • Carnot work: $\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - T_L/T_H = 1 - 303.15/523.15 = 0.42056$.
    $\dot{W}_{Carnot} = \eta_{Carnot} \times \dot{Q}_H = 0.42056 \times 6 = 2.523$ kW.
  • Carnot heat rejected: $|\dot{Q}_L|_{Carnot} = \dot{Q}_H - \dot{W}_{Carnot} = 6 - 2.523 = 3.477$ kW.
  • Clausius Check: $\frac{6}{523.15} - \frac{3.477}{303.15} = 0.01147 - 0.01147 = 0$.
  • This **satisfies** the Clausius Inequality as an equality (Reversible).
