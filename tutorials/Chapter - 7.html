Question 1: Jet Engine Diffuser

Question:

Atmospheric air at $-45$°C and $60$ kPa enters the front diffuser section of a jet engine with a velocity of $900$ km/h. The inlet frontal area is $1$ m². After passing through the adiabatic diffuser, the air velocity is reduced to $20$ m/s. Assuming the process is reversible (isentropic) and that air behaves as an ideal gas with constant specific heats, determine:
a) The temperature of the air at the diffuser exit ($T_2$).
b) The maximum possible pressure at the diffuser exit ($P_2$).
c) Represent the process on P-v and T-s diagrams.

Final Answer:

a) The diffuser exit temperature is $T_2 \approx 259.1$ K ($-14.1$ °C).
b) The maximum possible exit pressure is $P_2 \approx 93.2$ kPa.
c) Diagrams are described below.

Full Solution:

1. Convert Units:

  • Inlet Temperature: $T_1 = -45$ °C $+ 273.15 = 228.15$ K
  • Inlet Velocity: $V_1 = 900 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}} \times \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{1 \text{ km}} \times \frac{1 \text{ h}}{3600 \text{ s}} = 250$ m/s

2. Apply Steady Flow Energy Equation (SFEE):

For a steady-flow diffuser, the energy balance is:

$$ \dot{q} + h_1 + \frac{V_1^2}{2} = h_2 + \frac{V_2^2}{2} + \dot{w} $$

Assumptions:

  • Adiabatic process: $\dot{q} = 0$
  • No work done: $\dot{w} = 0$
  • Negligible potential energy change.

The SFEE simplifies to: $h_1 + \frac{V_1^2}{2} = h_2 + \frac{V_2^2}{2}$

Rearranging: $h_2 - h_1 = \frac{V_1^2 - V_2^2}{2}$

For an ideal gas with constant specific heat: $h_2 - h_1 = C_p(T_2 - T_1)$

So, $C_p(T_2 - T_1) = \frac{V_1^2 - V_2^2}{2}$

3. Calculate Exit Temperature ($T_2$):

  • $V_1 = 250$ m/s
  • $V_2 = 20$ m/s
  • $C_p = 1.004$ kJ/kg-K $= 1004$ J/kg-K (Table A.5)
$$ T_2 = T_1 + \frac{V_1^2 - V_2^2}{2 C_p} $$ $$ T_2 = 228.15 \text{ K} + \frac{(250 \text{ m/s})^2 - (20 \text{ m/s})^2}{2 \times 1004 \text{ J/kg-K}} $$ $$ T_2 = 228.15 \text{ K} + \frac{62500 - 400}{2008} \frac{\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2}{\text{J/kg-K}} $$ $$ T_2 = 228.15 \text{ K} + \frac{62100}{2008} \text{ K} $$ $$ T_2 = 228.15 \text{ K} + 30.926 \text{ K} $$ $$ T_2 = 259.076 \text{ K} \approx 259.1 \text{ K} $$

4. Calculate Exit Pressure ($P_2$):

The process is reversible and adiabatic, hence isentropic ($s_2 = s_1$). For an ideal gas with constant specific heats undergoing an isentropic process:

$$ \frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)^{\frac{k-1}{k}} $$

Rearranging for $P_2$:

$$ P_2 = P_1 \left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^{\frac{k}{k-1}} $$
  • $k = 1.4$ (Table A.5)
  • $k/(k-1) = 1.4 / (1.4 - 1) = 1.4 / 0.4 = 3.5$
  • $P_1 = 60$ kPa
$$ P_2 = 60 \text{ kPa} \times \left(\frac{259.076 \text{ K}}{228.15 \text{ K}}\right)^{3.5} $$ $$ P_2 = 60 \text{ kPa} \times (1.13555)^{3.5} $$ $$ P_2 = 60 \text{ kPa} \times 1.5538 $$ $$ P_2 = 93.228 \text{ kPa} \approx 93.2 \text{ kPa} $$

5. Process Representation:

  • T-s Diagram: The process is isentropic, so it is represented by a vertical line segment pointing upwards (entropy $s$ is constant, temperature $T$ increases from $T_1$ to $T_2$). Lines of constant pressure would show $P_2 > P_1$.
  • P-v Diagram: The process follows $Pv^k = \text{constant}$. It is represented by a curve moving from low pressure ($P_1$) and high specific volume ($v_1$) to high pressure ($P_2$) and low specific volume ($v_2$).

Table Values Used Summary:

  • Air: $C_p = 1.004$ kJ/kg-K, $k = 1.4$ (from Table A.5)

Question 2: Reversible Heat Pump Work

Question:

A steady flow of $2$ kg/s of R-410A, initially saturated vapour at $500$ kPa, is heated at constant pressure to a final temperature of $60$°C. This heating is achieved using a heat pump that absorbs heat from the ambient surroundings at $300$ K and requires a work input. Assuming all processes are reversible, determine the rate of work input required by the heat pump.

Final Answer:

The rate of work input required by the heat pump is $3.95$ kW.

Full Solution:

1. Determine State 1 Properties (Saturated Vapor at 500 kPa):

Interpolate in Table B.4.1 between $P=480.4$ kPa ($T=-15$°C) and $P=573.1$ kPa ($T=-10$°C):

  • $h_1 = 273.90 + \frac{500 - 480.4}{573.1 - 480.4} \times (275.78 - 273.90) = 273.90 + 0.397 = 274.30$ kJ/kg
  • $s_1 = 1.0671 + \frac{500 - 480.4}{573.1 - 480.4} \times (1.0567 - 1.0671) = 1.0671 - 0.0022 = 1.0649$ kJ/kg-K

2. Determine State 2 Properties (Superheated at 500 kPa, 60°C):

Interpolate in Table B.4.2 between $P=400$ kPa and $P=600$ kPa at $T_2=60$°C:

  • $h_2 = 343.58 + \frac{500 - 400}{600 - 400} \times (341.05 - 343.58) = 343.58 - 1.265 = 342.315$ kJ/kg
  • $s_2 = 1.3242 + \frac{500 - 400}{600 - 400} \times (1.2722 - 1.3242) = 1.3242 - 0.026 = 1.2982$ kJ/kg-K

3. Calculate Heat Transfer Rate to R-410A ($\dot{Q}_H$):

This is the heat output from the heat pump to the R-410A.

$$ \dot{Q}_H = \dot{m}(h_2 - h_1) = 2 \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{s}} \times (342.315 - 274.30) \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} $$ $$ \dot{Q}_H = 2 \times 68.015 = 136.03 \text{ kW} $$

4. Calculate Reversible Work Input ($\dot{W}_{HP}$):

For a reversible process heating the stream from state 1 to 2 using heat from ambient $T_0=300$ K, the minimum work input is equal to the increase in the flow exergy of the stream (since exergy of heat from ambient is zero).

$$ \dot{W}_{HP, rev} = \dot{m}(\psi_2 - \psi_1) $$

Where flow exergy change $\psi_2 - \psi_1 = (h_2 - h_1) - T_0(s_2 - s_1)$.

$$ \dot{W}_{HP, rev} = \dot{m}[(h_2 - h_1) - T_0(s_2 - s_1)] $$ $$ \dot{W}_{HP, rev} = 2 \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{s}} \times [ (342.315 - 274.30) \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} - 300 \text{ K} \times (1.2982 - 1.0649) \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} ] $$ $$ \dot{W}_{HP, rev} = 2 \times [ 68.015 - 300 \times 0.2333 ] \text{ kW} $$ $$ \dot{W}_{HP, rev} = 2 \times [ 68.015 - 69.99 ] \text{ kW} $$ $$ \dot{W}_{HP, rev} = 2 \times [-1.975] \text{ kW} = -3.95 \text{ kW} $$

The negative sign indicates that this work is done *on* the system (input). Conventionally, work input is positive.

Therefore, the rate of work input required is $\dot{W}_{HP} = 3.95$ kW.

(Alternative check: $Q_L = T_0 \dot{m}(s_2-s_1) = 300 \times 2 \times 0.2333 = 139.98$ kW. $W = Q_H - Q_L = 136.03 - 139.98 = -3.95$ kW.)

Table Values Used Summary:

  • R-410A Tables B.4.1 & B.4.2 for $h_1, s_1, h_2, s_2$.

Question 3: Surrounding Temperature from Exergy Change

Question:

Consider an adiabatic steady-state process where the change in enthalpy ($\Delta h$) is $7200$ kJ/kg, the change in flow exergy ($\Delta \psi$) is $4800$ kJ/kg, and the change in entropy ($\Delta s$) is $8$ kJ/kg-K. Kinetic and potential energy changes are negligible. Determine the temperature of the surroundings ($T_0$).

Final Answer:

The surrounding temperature ($T_0$) is $300$ K.

Full Solution:

The change in specific flow exergy ($\psi$) for a steady-state process, neglecting kinetic and potential energy changes, is defined as:

$$ \Delta\psi = (h_2 - h_1) - T_0(s_2 - s_1) $$ $$ \Delta\psi = \Delta h - T_0\Delta s $$

Where:

  • $\Delta\psi$ is the change in specific flow exergy (kJ/kg)
  • $\Delta h$ is the change in specific enthalpy (kJ/kg)
  • $T_0$ is the absolute temperature of the surroundings (K)
  • $\Delta s$ is the change in specific entropy (kJ/kg-K)

Given values:

  • $\Delta h = 7200$ kJ/kg
  • $\Delta \psi = 4800$ kJ/kg
  • $\Delta s = 8$ kJ/kg-K

We need to find $T_0$. Rearrange the exergy equation to solve for $T_0$:

$$ T_0\Delta s = \Delta h - \Delta \psi $$ $$ T_0 = \frac{\Delta h - \Delta \psi}{\Delta s} $$

Substitute the given values:

$$ T_0 = \frac{7200 \text{ kJ/kg} - 4800 \text{ kJ/kg}}{8 \text{ kJ/kg-K}} $$ $$ T_0 = \frac{2400 \text{ kJ/kg}}{8 \text{ kJ/kg-K}} $$

Calculation:

$$ T_0 = 300 \text{ K} $$

Calculation Check 1: $(7200 - 4800) / 8 = 2400 / 8 = 300$ K. Units: (kJ/kg) / (kJ/kg-K) = K. Correct.

Calculation Check 2: Verify the original equation: $\Delta \psi = 7200 - 300 \times 8 = 7200 - 2400 = 4800$ kJ/kg. Matches the given value.

The surrounding temperature $T_0$ is $300$ K.

Table Values Used: None required.

Question 4: Reversible Adiabatic Air Turbine with Mixing

Question:

An adiabatic air turbine receives two inlet streams: Stream 1 enters at $1$ kg/s, $1500$ K, and $1.6$ MPa. Stream 2 enters at $2$ kg/s, $400$ kPa, and an unknown temperature $T_2$. The mixture exits the turbine at $100$ kPa. Assuming the entire process within the turbine is reversible and air behaves as an ideal gas (use air tables), determine the temperature $T_2$ required.

Final Answer:

The required temperature $T_2$ is approximately $1072$ K.

Full Solution:

1. System Definition: Control volume is the adiabatic turbine.

2. Assumptions: Steady-state, adiabatic ($\dot{Q}=0$), reversible ($\dot{S}_{gen}=0$), air ideal gas (use Table A.7), negligible KE/PE changes.

3. Governing Equations:

  • Mass Balance: $\dot{m}_3 = \dot{m}_1 + \dot{m}_2 = 1 + 2 = 3$ kg/s
  • Entropy Balance (Reversible, Adiabatic): $\dot{S}_{gen} = \sum \dot{m}_e s_e - \sum \dot{m}_i s_i = 0$
    $\dot{m}_3 s_3 - (\dot{m}_1 s_1 + \dot{m}_2 s_2) = 0$
    $3 s_3 = 1 s_1 + 2 s_2$

4. Reversible Process Condition & Interpretation:

For the overall process including mixing and expansion to be reversible, the entropy generation must be zero. If streams 1 and 2 mix at different states ($T$ or $P$), the mixing process itself is inherently irreversible. The only way for the *entire* process (mixing + expansion) to be idealized as reversible overall is if the mixing itself causes no entropy generation, which requires the streams to be at the same state before mixing, OR if the problem implies we must choose $T_2$ such that the specific entropy of stream 2 matches that of stream 1 ($s_2 = s_1$). This forces the mixed stream entering the conceptual "expansion" part to have a uniform specific entropy equal to $s_1$ (and $s_2$), allowing the subsequent expansion to $P_3$ to also be isentropic ($s_3=s_1=s_2$). We proceed with the assumption $s_2 = s_1$.

5. Calculate $s_1$:

Use ideal gas entropy relation: $s = s^\circ(T) - R \ln(P/P_{ref})$. Let $P_{ref} = 100$ kPa = $0.1$ MPa.

  • $T_1 = 1500$ K. From Table A.7.1: $s^\circ(1500 \text{ K}) = 8.61208$ kJ/kg-K.
  • $P_1 = 1.6$ MPa $= 1600$ kPa. $P_1/P_{ref} = 16$.
  • $R_{air} = 0.287$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5).
$$ s_1 = 8.61208 - 0.287 \ln(16) = 8.61208 - 0.287 \times 2.7726 $$ $$ s_1 = 8.61208 - 0.7957 = 7.8164 \text{ kJ/kg-K} $$

6. Calculate $T_2$ assuming $s_2 = s_1$:

  • $s_2 = s^\circ(T_2) - R \ln(P_2/P_{ref})$
  • $P_2 = 400$ kPa. $P_2/P_{ref} = 4$.
$$ 7.8164 = s^\circ(T_2) - 0.287 \ln(4) $$ $$ 7.8164 = s^\circ(T_2) - 0.287 \times 1.3863 $$ $$ 7.8164 = s^\circ(T_2) - 0.3978 $$ $$ s^\circ(T_2) = 7.8164 + 0.3978 = 8.2142 \text{ kJ/kg-K} $$

7. Find $T_2$ by Interpolation (Table A.7.1):

$s^\circ(T_2) = 8.2142$ lies between $s^\circ(1050 \text{ K}) = 8.19081$ and $s^\circ(1100 \text{ K}) = 8.24449$.

$$ T_2 = 1050 \text{ K} + \frac{8.2142 - 8.19081}{8.24449 - 8.19081} \times (1100 \text{ K} - 1050 \text{ K}) $$ $$ T_2 = 1050 + \frac{0.02339}{0.05368} \times 50 $$ $$ T_2 = 1050 + 0.4357 \times 50 = 1050 + 21.79 \approx 1071.8 \text{ K} $$

Rounding, $T_2 \approx 1072$ K.

Table Values Used Summary:

  • Air: $R = 0.287$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5). Table A.7.1 for $s^\circ$ values at $1500$ K, $1050$ K, $1100$ K.

Question 5: Tank Discharge Entropy Generation

Question:

A tank with a volume of $0.002$ m³ initially contains air at $300$ kPa and $400$ K. A valve is opened, allowing some air to escape to the ambient surroundings until the pressure inside the tank drops to $200$ kPa. During this process, the tank is heated such that the temperature of the air remaining inside remains constant at $400$ K. Assuming the process is internally reversible, determine the mass-averaged specific entropy of the air that escaped the tank.

Final Answer:

The mass-averaged specific entropy of the leaving air is $s_{e,avg} \approx 6.897$ kJ/kg-K.

Full Solution:

1. System: Control volume enclosing the tank.

2. Process: Unsteady flow, mass leaving, heat added, constant temperature inside $T=400$ K. Internally reversible ($S_{gen}=0$).

3. States: Air is Ideal Gas.

  • Initial (1): $P_1 = 300$ kPa, $T_1 = 400$ K, $V_1 = 0.002$ m³
  • Final (2): $P_2 = 200$ kPa, $T_2 = 400$ K, $V_2 = 0.002$ m³

4. Mass Calculations: $m = PV / (RT)$

  • $R_{air} = 0.287$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5)
  • $m_1 = \frac{300 \times 0.002}{0.287 \times 400} = \frac{0.6}{114.8} = 5.2265$ kg
  • $m_2 = \frac{200 \times 0.002}{0.287 \times 400} = \frac{0.4}{114.8} = 3.4843$ kg
  • Mass escaped: $m_e = m_1 - m_2 = 5.2265 - 3.4843 = 1.7422$ kg

5. Property Values at T = 400 K: (Table A.7.1)

  • $u_1 = u_2 = u = 286.49$ kJ/kg
  • $h_1 = h_2 = h = 401.30$ kJ/kg
  • $s^\circ = 7.15926$ kJ/kg-K
  • Enthalpy of escaping air $h_{e,avg} = h = 401.30$ kJ/kg (since T is constant)

Calculate specific entropies inside: $s = s^\circ(T) - R \ln(P/P_{ref})$, $P_{ref}=100$ kPa.

  • $s_1 = 7.15926 - 0.287 \ln(300/100) = 7.15926 - 0.3153 = 6.8440$ kJ/kg-K
  • $s_2 = 7.15926 - 0.287 \ln(200/100) = 7.15926 - 0.1989 = 6.9604$ kJ/kg-K

6. Energy Balance (Unsteady Flow): $\Delta U_{cv} = Q_{cv} - W_{cv} - \int \dot{m}_e h_e dt$

$U_2 - U_1 = Q_{cv} - 0 - m_e h_{e,avg}$ (W=0, E=U)

$m_2 u_2 - m_1 u_1 = Q_{cv} - m_e h$

Since $u_1 = u_2 = u$ and $h_{e,avg} = h$: $(m_2 - m_1)u = Q_{cv} - m_e h$

$-m_e u = Q_{cv} - m_e h \implies Q_{cv} = m_e (h - u) = m_e (RT)$

$$ Q_{cv} = 1.7422 \text{ kg} \times 0.287 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times 400 \text{ K} = 199.98 \text{ kJ} \approx 200 \text{ kJ} $$

7. Entropy Balance (Unsteady Flow): $\Delta S_{cv} = \int \frac{\dot{Q}_{cv}}{T_b} dt - \int \dot{m}_e s_e dt + S_{gen}$

$S_2 - S_1 = \frac{Q_{cv}}{T} - m_e s_{e,avg} + 0$ (T_b = T = 400K, internally reversible $S_{gen}=0$)

$m_2 s_2 - m_1 s_1 = \frac{Q_{cv}}{T} - m_e s_{e,avg}$

Rearrange for $s_{e,avg}$:

$$ m_e s_{e,avg} = \frac{Q_{cv}}{T} - (m_2 s_2 - m_1 s_1) $$ $$ s_{e,avg} = \frac{Q_{cv}}{m_e T} - \frac{m_2 s_2 - m_1 s_1}{m_e} $$

Substitute values:

  • $\frac{Q_{cv}}{m_e T} = \frac{m_e R T}{m_e T} = R = 0.287$ kJ/kg-K
  • $m_2 s_2 = 3.4843 \times 6.9604 = 24.2529$ kJ/K
  • $m_1 s_1 = 5.2265 \times 6.8440 = 35.7695$ kJ/K
  • $\frac{m_2 s_2 - m_1 s_1}{m_e} = \frac{24.2529 - 35.7695}{1.7422} = \frac{-11.5166}{1.7422} = -6.6103$ kJ/kg-K
$$ s_{e,avg} = 0.287 - (-6.6103) = 0.287 + 6.6103 = 6.8973 \text{ kJ/kg-K} $$

Rounding, $s_{e,avg} \approx 6.897$ kJ/kg-K.

Table Values Used Summary:

  • Air: $R=0.287$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5). Table A.7.1 for $u, h, s^\circ$ at 400 K.

Question 6: Compressor Work for Tank Charging

Question:

An initially evacuated, insulated tank of $2$ m³ volume is charged with R-134a from a supply line carrying the refrigerant at $3$ MPa. The valve is closed when the pressure inside the tank reaches $3$ MPa. The supply line is fed by an insulated, reversible compressor that takes in R-134a at $5$°C with a quality of $96.5\%$ and compresses it to $3$ MPa. Calculate the total work input to the compressor required to charge the tank.

Final Answer:

The total work input to the compressor is $W_{comp,total} \approx 11.6$ MJ.

Full Solution:

1. Compressor Analysis (Steady, Adiabatic, Reversible = Isentropic):

  • Inlet State (c1): $T_{c1} = 5$°C, $x_{c1} = 0.965$. From Table B.5.1 at 5°C ($P_{sat}=350.9$ kPa):
    $h_{c1} = h_f + x_{c1} h_{fg} = 206.75 + 0.965 \times 194.57 = 206.75 + 187.76 = 394.51$ kJ/kg
    $s_{c1} = s_f + x_{c1} s_{fg} = 1.0243 + 0.965 \times 0.6995 = 1.0243 + 0.6750 = 1.6993$ kJ/kg-K
  • Outlet State (c2 / Line State): $P_{c2} = 3$ MPa $= 3000$ kPa, $s_{c2} = s_{c1} = 1.6993$ kJ/kg-K.
    Check Table B.5.2 at $P=3000$ kPa: $s_g = 1.6763$ kJ/kg-K. Since $s_{c2} > s_g$, it's superheated.
    Interpolate between $T=90$°C ($s=1.6861$) and $T=100$°C ($s=1.7141$) for $h_{c2}$: $$ h_{c2} = 425.70 + \frac{1.6993 - 1.6861}{1.7141 - 1.6861} \times (438.56 - 425.70) $$ $$ h_{c2} = 425.70 + \frac{0.0132}{0.0280} \times 12.86 = 425.70 + 6.06 = 431.76 \text{ kJ/kg} $$
  • Compressor specific work: $w_{comp} = h_{c2} - h_{c1} = 431.76 - 394.51 = 37.25$ kJ/kg

2. Tank Filling Analysis (Unsteady):

  • System: Tank (Control Volume). $Q=0, W=0$.
  • Initial state (t1): Evacuated, $m_{t1}=0, U_{t1}=0$.
  • Final state (t2): $P_{t2} = 3$ MPa.
  • Mass balance: $m_{t2} - m_{t1} = m_{in} \implies m_{t2} = m_{in}$
  • Energy balance: $U_{t2} - U_{t1} = m_{in} h_{line} \implies m_{t2} u_{t2} = m_{t2} h_{c2} \implies u_{t2} = h_{c2}$
  • Final state in tank: $P_{t2} = 3000$ kPa, $u_{t2} = h_{c2} = 431.76$ kJ/kg.
  • Find $v_{t2}$ by interpolating in Table B.5.2 at $P=3000$ kPa for $u=431.76$:
    Interpolate between $T=110$°C ($u=429.78, v=0.006323$) and $T=120$°C ($u=442.53, v=0.006835$): $$ v_{t2} = 0.006323 + \frac{431.76 - 429.78}{442.53 - 429.78} \times (0.006835 - 0.006323) $$ $$ v_{t2} = 0.006323 + \frac{1.98}{12.75} \times 0.000512 = 0.006323 + 0.0000795 = 0.006403 \text{ m³/kg} $$
  • Final mass in tank: $m_{t2} = V / v_{t2} = 2 \text{ m³} / 0.006403 \text{ m³/kg} = 312.35$ kg
  • Total mass compressed: $m_{in} = m_{t2} = 312.35$ kg

3. Total Compressor Work:

$$ W_{comp,total} = m_{in} \times w_{comp} = 312.35 \text{ kg} \times 37.25 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} $$ $$ W_{comp,total} = 11635.5 \text{ kJ} \approx 11.6 \text{ MJ} $$

Table Values Used Summary:

  • R-134a Tables B.5.1 & B.5.2 for $h, s, u, v$ at various states.

Question 7: Compressor Entropy Generation

Question:

A compressor in a commercial refrigerator operates with R-410A. The refrigerant enters as saturated vapor ($x=1$) at $-25$°C. The exit conditions are $2000$ kPa and $80$°C. Neglecting kinetic energies, calculate the specific entropy generation during this process.

Final Answer:

The specific entropy generation is $s_{gen} = 0.0480$ kJ/kg-K.

Full Solution:

1. Identify States:

  • Inlet State (1): $T_1 = -25$°C, $x_1 = 1$ (saturated vapor).
  • Exit State (2): $P_2 = 2000$ kPa, $T_2 = 80$°C.

2. Find Entropy at State 1:

From Table B.4.1 for saturated R-410A at $T_1 = -25$°C:

  • $s_1 = s_g = 1.0893$ kJ/kg-K

3. Find Entropy at State 2:

State 2 is defined by $P_2 = 2000$ kPa and $T_2 = 80$°C. Check Table B.4.2 (Superheated R-410A). At $P=2000$ kPa ($2$ MPa), $T_{sat} = 32.31$°C. Since $T_2 > T_{sat}$, the state is superheated.

From Table B.4.2 at $P=2000$ kPa and $T=80$°C:

  • $s_2 = 1.1373$ kJ/kg-K

4. Calculate Specific Entropy Generation:

Apply the entropy balance for a steady-flow control volume:

$$ \frac{dS_{cv}}{dt} = \sum (\frac{\dot{Q}}{T})_b + \sum \dot{m}_i s_i - \sum \dot{m}_e s_e + \dot{S}_{gen} $$

For steady state ($dS_{cv}/dt = 0$) and assuming the compressor is adiabatic ($\dot{Q}=0$):

$$ 0 = \dot{m}s_1 - \dot{m}s_2 + \dot{S}_{gen} $$ $$ \dot{S}_{gen} = \dot{m}(s_2 - s_1) $$

The specific entropy generation (per unit mass) is:

$$ s_{gen} = \frac{\dot{S}_{gen}}{\dot{m}} = s_2 - s_1 $$ $$ s_{gen} = 1.1373 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} - 1.0893 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} $$ $$ s_{gen} = 0.0480 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} $$

Table Values Used Summary:

  • R-410A Tables B.4.1 & B.4.2 for $s_1, s_2$.

Question 8: Heat Exchanger Entropy Generation

Question:

A counter-flow heat exchanger operates steadily. One line carries air ($\dot{m}_{air} = 2$ kg/s) entering at $125$ kPa, $1000$ K and leaving at $100$ kPa, $400$ K. The other line carries water ($\dot{m}_{water} = 0.5$ kg/s) entering at $200$ kPa, $20$°C and leaving at $200$ kPa. Assuming the heat exchanger is adiabatic, determine:
a) The exit temperature of the water ($T_{w4}$).
b) The total rate of entropy generation ($\dot{S}_{gen}$).

Final Answer:

a) The water exit temperature is $T_{w4} = 120.23$ °C (saturated mixture).
b) The total rate of entropy generation is $\dot{S}_{gen} \approx 1.54$ kW/K.

Full Solution:

1. Energy Balance:

For a steady-state, adiabatic heat exchanger with no work: $\sum \dot{m}_i h_i = \sum \dot{m}_e h_e$

$$ \dot{m}_{air}h_{a1} + \dot{m}_{water}h_{w3} = \dot{m}_{air}h_{a2} + \dot{m}_{water}h_{w4} $$

2. Get Properties:

  • Air (Table A.7.1): $h_{a1}(1000K) = 1046.22$ kJ/kg, $h_{a2}(400K) = 401.30$ kJ/kg.

3. Calculate $h_{w4}$:

4. Determine Water Exit Temperature ($T_{w4}$):

Find state at $P_{w4}=200$ kPa, $h_{w4}=2663.62$ kJ/kg.

From Table B.1.2 at 200 kPa: $h_f=504.68$, $h_g=2706.63$ kJ/kg.

Since $h_f < h_{w4} < h_g$, state w4 is a saturated mixture.

$T_{w4} = T_{sat}(200 \text{ kPa}) = 120.23$ °C.

5. Entropy Generation Rate:

$$ \dot{S}_{gen} = \sum \dot{m}_e s_e - \sum \dot{m}_i s_i \quad (\text{Adiabatic, Steady}) $$ $$ \dot{S}_{gen} = (\dot{m}_{air}s_{a2} + \dot{m}_{water}s_{w4}) - (\dot{m}_{air}s_{a1} + \dot{m}_{water}s_{w3}) $$
  • Calculate air entropies: $s = s^\circ(T) - R \ln(P/P_{ref})$, $P_{ref}=100$ kPa, $R=0.287$ kJ/kg-K.
    $s_{a1} = s^\circ(1000\text{K}) - R \ln(125/100) = 8.13493 - 0.287\ln(1.25) = 8.13493 - 0.0640 = 8.0709$ kJ/kg-K

    $S_{gen,12} = 2.323 (1.0971 - 1.0009) - \frac{63.27}{293.15} = 2.323 \times 0.0962 - 0.2158$
    $S_{gen,12} = 0.2235 - 0.2158 = 0.0077$ kJ/K.
$$ S_{gen,total} = S_{gen,01} + S_{gen,12} = 0.0021 + 0.0077 = 0.0098 \text{ kJ/K} $$

Question 11: Irrigation Pump Max Flow Rate

Question:

An irrigation pump takes water from a river at $10$°C, $100$ kPa. It pumps the water to an open canal located $100$ m higher in elevation. The pipe diameter is $0.1$ m at both the inlet and outlet of the pump. The motor driving the pump supplies $5$ hp of power. Neglecting friction and kinetic energy changes across the pump, find the maximum possible mass flow rate of the water.

Final Answer:

The maximum possible mass flow rate is approximately $3.80$ kg/s.

Full Solution:

1. Define System & Process: System is water from river surface (1) to canal surface (2). Max flow rate implies reversible process (no friction losses considered internal to pump or in lifting).

2. States:

  • State 1 (River): $T_1=10$°C, $P_1=100$ kPa, $z_1=0$ (ref), $V_1 \approx 0$.
  • State 2 (Canal): $P_2=100$ kPa, $z_2=100$ m, $V_2 \approx 0$.
  • Water incompressible liquid: $v \approx v_f(10°C) \approx 0.001000$ m³/kg.

3. Power Conversion:

  • $\dot{W}_{shaft} = 5 \text{ hp} \times 0.7457 \frac{\text{kW}}{\text{hp}} = 3.7285 \text{ kW} = 3728.5 \text{ J/s}$.

4. Energy Balance (Overall System 1 -> 2):

Apply SFEE between free surfaces:

$$ \dot{m}(h_1 + \frac{V_1^2}{2} + gz_1) + \dot{W}_{shaft} = \dot{m}(h_2 + \frac{V_2^2}{2} + gz_2) $$

With $V_1 \approx 0, V_2 \approx 0, z_1=0$:

$$ \dot{m}h_1 + \dot{W}_{shaft} = \dot{m}h_2 + \dot{m}gz_2 $$ $$ \dot{W}_{shaft} = \dot{m}(h_2 - h_1 + gz_2) $$

5. Reversible Pump Work for Incompressible Fluid:

For an incompressible fluid, the reversible specific work done by a pump in steady flow is approximately $w_{rev} = v(P_2 - P_1) + g(z_2 - z_1)$ (ignoring KE). In this overall process $P_1 = P_2$, so the only reversible work needed is to increase potential energy.

Alternatively, for the overall process from river surface to canal surface, the minimum required shaft power (reversible process) goes entirely into increasing the potential energy, as kinetic energy changes are negligible and pressure is the same.

$$ \dot{W}_{shaft, rev} = \dot{m} g (z_2 - z_1) $$

6. Calculate Maximum Mass Flow Rate:

Maximum flow occurs when all supplied shaft power is used ideally (reversibly) to lift the water.

$$ \dot{m}_{max} = \frac{\dot{W}_{shaft}}{g(z_2 - z_1)} $$ $$ \dot{m}_{max} = \frac{3728.5 \text{ J/s}}{9.80665 \text{ m/s²} \times 100 \text{ m}} $$ $$ \dot{m}_{max} = \frac{3728.5}{980.665} \frac{\text{J/s}}{\text{m²/s²}} = 3.802 \text{ kg/s} $$

Rounding, $\dot{m}_{max} \approx 3.80$ kg/s.

Question 12: Pipe Draining Entropy Generation

Question:

A $10$ m tall pipe with a $0.1$ m diameter is initially filled with liquid water at $20$°C. The top is open to the atmosphere ($100$ kPa). A small nozzle is mounted at the bottom. Water is allowed to flow out through the nozzle, splashing onto the ground (at the same level as the nozzle exit) until the pipe is empty. Assuming the process occurs reasonably quickly but the water on the ground eventually returns to $20$°C, find:
a) The initial exit velocity of the water from the nozzle.
b) The average specific kinetic energy of the water in the exit flow.
c) The total entropy generation for the entire process.

Final Answer:

a) Initial exit velocity $V_{exit,initial} \approx 14.0$ m/s.
b) Average specific kinetic energy $KE_{avg} \approx 49.0$ J/kg (or $0.049$ kJ/kg).
c) Total entropy generation $S_{gen,total} \approx 0.0131$ kJ/K.

Full Solution:

1. Initial State: Water column $H=10$ m, $D=0.1$ m, $T=20$°C, $P_{top}=100$ kPa. $\rho \approx 1/v_f(20°C) \approx 1/0.001002 \approx 998$ kg/m³.

2. Initial Exit Velocity ($V_{initial}$):

Apply Bernoulli's equation between top surface (1) and nozzle exit (2):

$$ \frac{P_1}{\rho} + \frac{V_1^2}{2} + gz_1 = \frac{P_2}{\rho} + \frac{V_2^2}{2} + gz_2 $$

Assume $P_1 = P_2 = P_{atm}$, $V_1 \approx 0$, $z_1=H=10$ m, $z_2=0$.

$$ gH = \frac{V_{initial}^2}{2} $$ $$ V_{initial} = \sqrt{2gH} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.80665 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s²}} \times 10 \text{ m}} = \sqrt{196.133} \approx 14.0 \text{ m/s} $$

3. Average Specific Kinetic Energy ($KE_{avg}$):

The instantaneous specific KE at exit is $V^2/2 = gh$, where $h$ is instantaneous height. The average specific KE of the *exiting* fluid corresponds to the average *potential energy* of the fluid initially in the pipe, relative to the exit level.

Total initial potential energy $PE_{total} = M g (H/2)$, where $M$ is total mass.

Average Specific PE = $PE_{total} / M = gH/2$.

This initial PE is converted to KE as the water exits and is then dissipated.

$$ KE_{avg} = \frac{gH}{2} = 9.80665 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s²}} \times \frac{10 \text{ m}}{2} = 49.033 \frac{\text{J}}{\text{kg}} \approx 49.0 \text{ J/kg} $$

4. Total Entropy Generation ($S_{gen,total}$):

System: Mass $M$ of water initially in the pipe.

  • Initial State (sys,1): $T_1=20$°C, average height $H/2$.
  • Final State (sys,2): $T_2=20$°C, height $z=0$ (on ground).
  • Surroundings: $T_{surr} = 20$°C = $293.15$ K.

Entropy Balance: $S_{gen,total} = \Delta S_{system} + \Delta S_{surroundings}$

  • $\Delta S_{system} = S_2 - S_1 = M(s_2 - s_1)$. Since $T_1=T_2$ and $P_1 \approx P_2 = P_{atm}$ (liquid), $s_1 \approx s_2$, so $\Delta S_{system} \approx 0$.
  • $\Delta S_{surroundings} = Q_{surr} / T_{surr}$. Heat $Q_{surr}$ is the energy dissipated from the water's initial potential energy as it comes to rest at $T_2=T_1$.
  • $Q_{surr}$ (heat absorbed by surroundings) = $PE_{total}$ (initial potential energy lost by water) = $M g (H/2)$.

Calculate Total Mass $M$:

$$ M = \rho \times \text{Volume} = \rho \times (\frac{\pi D^2}{4}) \times H $$ $$ M \approx 998 \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m³}} \times (\frac{\pi (0.1 \text{ m})^2}{4}) \times 10 \text{ m} \approx 78.38 \text{ kg} $$

Calculate Heat Transfer $Q_{surr}$:

$$ Q_{surr} = M \times KE_{avg} = 78.38 \text{ kg} \times 49.033 \frac{\text{J}}{\text{kg}} \approx 3843 \text{ J} = 3.843 \text{ kJ} $$

Calculate Total Entropy Generation:

$$ S_{gen,total} = \Delta S_{system} + \Delta S_{surroundings} \approx 0 + \frac{Q_{surr}}{T_{surr}} $$ $$ S_{gen,total} \approx \frac{3.843 \text{ kJ}}{293.15 \text{ K}} = 0.01311 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{K}} \approx 0.0131 \text{ kJ/K} $$

Table Values Used Summary:

  • $g = 9.80665$ m/s² (Table A.0a).
  • $v_f(20°C)$ for density (Table B.1.1).

Question 13: Compressor Work Reduction

Question:

Consider a reversible adiabatic air compressor compressing air from $P_1 = 100$ kPa to a fixed final pressure $P_2$. Compare the specific work required if the inlet temperature is $T_{A1} = 300$ K versus $T_{B1} = 280$ K. How much is the reduction in specific work (in kJ/kg) when the inlet temperature is lowered from $300$ K to $280$ K? Assume air is an ideal gas with constant specific heats.

Final Answer:

The reduction in specific work depends on the final pressure $P_2$. The reduction is given by $\Delta w = 20.08 \times [(\frac{P_2}{100 \text{ kPa}})^{0.2857} - 1]$ kJ/kg. If the question intended to ask for the reduction assuming the same *exit temperature* ($T_2$) was reached (which would require different $P_2$), the reduction would be $20.08$ kJ/kg.

Full Solution:

1. Process: Reversible adiabatic (isentropic) compression, ideal gas, constant $C_p$.

2. Specific Work: $w = h_2 - h_1 = C_p(T_2 - T_1)$.

3. Isentropic Relation: $\frac{T_2}{T_1} = (\frac{P_2}{P_1})^{\frac{k-1}{k}}$. Let pressure ratio $r_p = P_2/P_1$ (constant). Let exponent $\alpha = (k-1)/k = 0.4/1.4 \approx 0.2857$.

$$ T_2 = T_1 (r_p)^{\alpha} $$

4. Work Expression: Substitute $T_2$ into work equation:

$$ w = C_p [T_1 (r_p)^{\alpha} - T_1] = C_p T_1 [(r_p)^{\alpha} - 1] $$

5. Calculate Work for Both Cases: Use $C_p = 1.004$ kJ/kg-K (Table A.5).

  • Case A: $T_{A1} = 300$ K. $w_A = 1.004 \times 300 \times [(r_p)^{0.2857} - 1] = 301.2 [(r_p)^{0.2857} - 1]$
  • Case B: $T_{B1} = 280$ K. $w_B = 1.004 \times 280 \times [(r_p)^{0.2857} - 1] = 281.12 [(r_p)^{0.2857} - 1]$

6. Reduction in Work ($\Delta w$): $\Delta w = w_A - w_B$

$$ \Delta w = (301.2 - 281.12) \times [(r_p)^{0.2857} - 1] $$ $$ \Delta w = 20.08 \times [(r_p)^{0.2857} - 1] \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg}} $$

7. Interpretation: The reduction $\Delta w$ depends on the pressure ratio $r_p = P_2/P_1$. Since $P_2$ is fixed but unspecified, $r_p$ is also fixed but unknown. The formula above gives the reduction in terms of $r_p$.

8. Alternative Interpretation (Assuming fixed $T_2$): If the question implies the comparison is for reaching the *same exit temperature* $T_2$ (which requires different $P_2$ values):

  • $w_A = C_p(T_2 - T_{A1})$
  • $w_B = C_p(T_2 - T_{B1})$
  • $\Delta w = w_A - w_B = C_p T_2 - C_p T_{A1} - (C_p T_2 - C_p T_{B1}) = C_p (T_{B1} - T_{A1})$
  • $\Delta w = 1.004 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times (280 \text{ K} - 300 \text{ K}) = 1.004 \times (-20) = -20.08$ kJ/kg
  • The reduction in work (a positive value) is $20.08$ kJ/kg.

Given the wording often used in thermodynamics problems, the fixed $T_2$ interpretation yielding $20.08$ kJ/kg might be what was intended, despite the "fixed final pressure" statement creating ambiguity.

Table Values Used Summary:

  • Air: $C_p = 1.004$ kJ/kg-K, $k = 1.4$ (Table A.5).

  • Actual Exit (2a): $V_{2a} = 200$ m/s, $T_{2a} = 20$°C $= 293.15$ K, $P_{2a} = 100$ kPa.
  • Ideal Exit (2s): $P_{2s} = 100$ kPa, $s_{2s} = s_1$.

3. Energy Balance ($V_1 \approx 0$):

$$ h_1 + 0 = h_{2a} + \frac{V_{2a}^2}{2} $$

For constant specific heats: $C_p T_1 = C_p T_{2a} + \frac{V_{2a}^2}{2}$

Solve for $T_1$: $T_1 = T_{2a} + \frac{V_{2a}^2}{2 C_p}$. Use $C_p = 1004$ J/kg-K.

$$ T_1 = 293.15 \text{ K} + \frac{(200 \text{ m/s})^2}{2 \times 1004 \text{ J/kg-K}} $$ $$ T_1 = 293.15 + \frac{40000}{2008} = 293.15 + 19.92 = 313.07 \text{ K} \approx 313.1 \text{ K} $$

4. Isentropic Efficiency ($\eta_n = 0.92$):

$$ \eta_n = \frac{\text{Actual KE change}}{\text{Isentropic KE change}} = \frac{V_{2a}^2 / 2}{V_{2s}^2 / 2} = \frac{h_1 - h_{2a}}{h_1 - h_{2s}} $$

We know the actual enthalpy drop from energy balance: $h_1 - h_{2a} = V_{2a}^2 / 2 = (200^2)/2 = 20000$ J/kg $= 20.0$ kJ/kg.

Calculate the ideal enthalpy drop:

$$ h_1 - h_{2s} = \frac{h_1 - h_{2a}}{\eta_n} = \frac{20.0 \text{ kJ/kg}}{0.92} = 21.739 \text{ kJ/kg} $$

5. Find Ideal Exit Temperature ($T_{2s}$):

$$ h_1 - h_{2s} = C_p (T_1 - T_{2s}) $$ $$ 21.739 \text{ kJ/kg} = 1.004 \frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{kg-K}} \times (313.07 \text{ K} - T_{2s}) $$ $$ T_1 - T_{2s} = \frac{21.739}{1.004} = 21.652 \text{ K} $$ $$ T_{2s} = T_1 - 21.652 = 313.07 - 21.652 = 291.42 \text{ K} $$

6. Find Inlet Pressure ($P_1$):

Use the isentropic relation between states 1 and 2s ($s_1 = s_{2s}$):

$$ \frac{T_{2s}}{T_1} = \left(\frac{P_{2s}}{P_1}\right)^{\frac{k-1}{k}} $$

Use $P_{2s} = P_{2a} = 100$ kPa and $(k-1)/k = 0.2857$.

$$ \frac{291.42}{313.07} = \left(\frac{100 \text{ kPa}}{P_1}\right)^{0.2857} $$ $$ 0.93084 = \left(\frac{100}{P_1}\right)^{0.2857} $$

Raise both sides to power $1/0.2857 \approx 3.5$:

$$ (0.93084)^{3.5} = \frac{100}{P_1} $$ $$ 0.7817 = \frac{100}{P_1} $$ $$ P_1 = \frac{100}{0.7817} = 127.93 \text{ kPa} \approx 128 \text{ kPa} $$
